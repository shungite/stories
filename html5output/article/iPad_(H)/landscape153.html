He then returned briefly to St. George’s until he was appointed as our organist. In Herb’s own words: “ In the early fall of 1919, a very self conscious young man, but lately returned from active service in France “aimed at the moon” by applying for the position of organist at Westminster Church … After an anxious period of waiting ( for this was the most important event in the applicant’s musical life so far), there came one day a call to try out … How often he had passed by that magnificent facade of Westminster Church, and dreamed that, somehow, one day, he might perhaps realize the secret, but nevertheless very real ambition that he might become the organist …” His official appointment as organist was made in May, 1920. By the mid-1920s Herb realized the need to complete his organ training. He thereafter studied, first under Hugh Ross and later under Douglas Clarke and Hugh Bancroft. He ultimately received both his ACCO ( Associate Canadian College of Organists) and FTCL (Fellow Trinity College London.) diplomas. He then became not only our man but a major ‘player’ in the musical life of Winnipeg. He was conductor of The Winnipeg Male Voice Choir-1935 to 1944, the Winnipeg Philharmonic Choir (with Filmer Hubble) from 1936 to 1945, and the 85-voice Metropolitan Choir (circa 1939-45) where, among other repertoire, he conducted Arthur Sullivan’s cantata The Golden Legend . A form of musical theatre was also to his liking with the Metropolitan Choir. They performed Scenes from the Song of Hiawatha  — Herb was the composer.
