As chair of the Membership and Visiting Committee she reorganized the district visiting with the help of Gordon Pattison. She was Secretary of the Pastoral Relations Committee and a team Captain for the Stewardship Campaign. Marilyn Huband who was a leader, both in the Church and in the wider community, and was a friend to many of us here today. She brought energy, joy and insight to the needs of Westminster. No new visitor to the Church went un noticed by Marilyn — she made a point of welcoming all. And Marilyn started the Mother’s Morning Off, here at the Church to provide space and time for women from the area to come together while their children were attended. She knew it was very important to involve young women in the affairs of the Church. Another memory that Georgine Kerr sent me is of her mother Marguerite (Bonnie) Kerr who joined Westminster in 1936. She attended Sunday school and CGIT. During the war years, she was a CGIT leader. She and Murray were active in the 50/50 Club. For many years she was a leader of the afternoon church circle. She was involved in the big Westminster church teas, held the first Wednesday of May, contributing baking. Onnumber of these, she was the convener for the tea. She was the chair of the Fellowship committee for many years. People described Bonnie as considerate, elegant, caring and friendly – and as Georgine says, Westminster was part of her life. There are many in our recent history that have been in the forefront of leadership in Westminster —  we continue to remember Betty Halstead and her support of Refugees. There is record by a refugee of Betty’s work — “Betty Halstead was in the heart of our efforts. Hers were the most difficult tasks, and she did them with great joy and enthusiasm. Betty was a true leader, with always positive approach, giving encouragement when needed, taking initiative when needed, breaking through cultural differences when needed.” There is also a choral scholarship in Betty’s name here at Westminster. And the work of Phyllis Reader and International Hope. Phyllis has worked tirelessly to collect and distribute surplus medical supplies to developing countries from Manitoba sources.
