Charles may have been an excellent Reverend, but his invitation by St. Andrew’s reflected these exigencies. Before we discuss Charles’ ministry here, another aside to further flesh out the scene to which he arrived: Readers will appreciate that in 1881 — two years before the birth of suffragette Nellie McClung and nearly 50 years before the Valiant Five petitioned for women to be considered legal persons, with all the liberating rights and dignifying duties that status entailed its bearer — society was still very much organized and dominated by men. There was then a discrete area of Point Douglas where women commanded, if nothing else, the desires and wallets of men, but otherwise it was a man’s world. So when the St. Andrew’s Society of Winnipeg — the oldest continuously operating Scottish society in western Canada — was founded in 1871, naturally it was a men’s only club. (In fact at the time of this writing, 2015, it still is.) So was The Manitoba Club — the province’s preeminent business and social club where, to quote its present website, “simple handshakes still close deals” — when it was formed in 1874, and it remained that way until recently. (Now the Club invites the sons and or daughters of Club members, with in excess of 10-years of membership, to join the Club with no entrance fee requirement.) After all, in 1874 women weren’t running businesses, at least not reputable ones. The Historical and Scientific Society of Manitoba was founded in 1879. No women there either, although the names of many of its founding men — such as Andrew Bannatyne, Joseph Hargrave, Colin Inkster, Edward Jarvis, John Norquay — resonate with us thanks to the streets named after them. As a point of ironical interest, the address at their opening dinner included this stirring peroration: “we are now waking up to the conviction, that our North-West is destined to be one of the most important parts of the globe  … and with the older eastern Provinces … will soon be the right arm of the British Empire.” Bold, but we aren’t quite there yet.
