For example, when we went to Westminster in 1963, the same man had been clerk of Session for a dozen years or more and the same person had held the position as chairman of Stewards for ten years. Also, there were only three or four women on these boards. To discuss possible changes, we organized a four-hour session, which we called a Westminster forum. This may have been where the idea was born, viz., to have one church board as the governing body of our congregation. Out of that idea we decided to have three groups meet—the executives of the UCW, of Stewards, and of Session—together with staff. It is not possible to describe all that happened in that year of monthly meetings. But it soon became apparent how much overlapping there was: e.g., both the UCW and the stewards had finance and property committees; both the Session and the UCW had a mission committee and a flower committee, to give only a couple of examples. As well, there were times when people had to report to both Stewards and Session, and maybe, as well, to the Official Board. It was a clumsy and an inefficient type of organization. But, in setting up a new board structure, we had to make certain that all the work of the congregation would be covered, yet not duplicated. Also, we wanted to make certain that there would be a good mix of women, men, and young people on the board. We wanted to be certain, too, that there would be a time limit for any one person to serve. We began the discussion and study of a possible new structure of congregational government at the beginning of 1969, and the final report was presented to the congregational meeting in January 1970. It was approved. The General Board (as we decided to call it) would be made up of nine committees, with four persons on each committee—worship, education, finance, property, communications, membership and visiting, fellowship, outreach, and nominations. There would also be a chairperson, vice chair, secretary, and treasurer, with the staff as ex officio members. Each board member would serve a four-year term, and the chair would change every second year. Each of the nine committees could add to their numbers for their own committee tasks, but all major decisions would be brought to the monthly meeting of the General Board for final approval. This obviously is a very brief description of a very major change in congregational government. (The text of the constitution is in my Westminster file.)
