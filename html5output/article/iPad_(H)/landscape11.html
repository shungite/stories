GEORGE READING CROWE, BUSINESSMAN AND POLITICIAN George was born at Truro, Nova Scotia on in October of 1852, and was educated in Truro public schools. He worked for his father in shipbuilding before striking out on a career in Canada’s bourgeoning railroad industry; moving to Manitoba in 1879 to work for CPR. A keenly enterprising man not content to limit himself to one industry, Crowe’s career covered what seems like every industry vital to Canada’s western expansion. After founding one lumber business with Nathaniel Boyd, he founded another — this one also specializing in grain — with his brother Herbert in 1890. After that business narrowed its focus exclusively to grain, George formed partnerships with other grain companies. Within five years, his reputation quickly cemented as an industry leader, he was appointed President of the Winnipeg Grain and Produce Exchange. From there, to the end of his career, we find him acting as a director on no less than eight boards of various companies in insurance, marble, grain, and banking. George was also a leader in the development of Canada’s Presbyterian church, beyond his role with Westminster. He was a commissioner of the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Canada, and in 1886 was appointed to the board of management of Manitoba College (then a Presbyterian institution, which would become one of the University of Manitoba’s founding colleges), serving as the College’s chairman from 1914 to 1918. An old-fashioned Tory, George found time to make politics as well: serving as a City Counsellor in 1885 and again from 1912 to 1914. His conservative outlook, was already apparent in 1904 when, in alliance with Rev. Frederick Du Val, minister of Knox Presbyterian Church, he worked to expel prostitution from the west end of Winnipeg. He arrived a businessman to Winnipeg at the right time, when nation-building projects friendly to private enterprise abounded, and bearing the right social markers to profit from those circumstances.
