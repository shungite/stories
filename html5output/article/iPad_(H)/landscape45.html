Their more orthodox opponents naturally accused them of Arminianism, and endeavoured to exclude them from the pulpits of Calvinist churches. This led many liberals to leave Congregationalism to join the Unitarian Church. Eventually, the Presbyterian and Methodist and Churches were both host to their own backlashes against the moderating and frankly secularizing influence of theological liberalism. For the Methodists this backlash is associated with the influence of Albert C. Outler, who helped to propagate a puritanical reinterpretation Wesleyianism that reasserted the latter’s emphasis on conversion and sanctification. For the Presbyterians, it’s associated with the rise of Presbyterian Fundmentalism, which sought to reaffirm certain fundamentals truths — such as the Virgin Birth and the resurrection  — that no liberal Presbyterian could prod with reasonable skepticism or scientific counterevidence. Thus, when the General Assembly of the Canadian Presbyterian Church of 1904 rolled around, all three Churches were moving in Evangelical directions — albeit with a common destination that, as it turned out, was still far more modern than where so many of their backwards Americans counterparts have ended up. These affinities born of the moment, combined with the Churches’ common nonconformist heritage, may have made talk of union less daunting, but the marriage was also one of convenience. Many towns in Canada lacked the pastoral manpower to accommodate congregations of all three churches, leading to informal associations between local Methodist, Presbyterian, and Congregationalist Churches, which were obliged to share ministers. This provided the practical basis for union, which, after years of discussion, was formalized in 1925 and inaugurated, in proper Evangelical fashion, with a massive, musically rich service in the arena of Mutual Street. (More on unification later.) It was also in the context of Canada’s more moderate Evangelical moment that Clarence MacKinnon became a particularly attractive candidate to lead Westminster Presbyterian Church in Winnipeg.
