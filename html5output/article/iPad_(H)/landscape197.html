Mr. Flipse was quiet, unassuming, very kind, very efficient, and, of course, extremely capable in terms of the church lawn. His hours were long; he was literally on call anytime, all the time, from when the doors opened in the morning until the last meeting was over at night. He had Monday evening and all day Tuesday off. His replacement for those times was old Mr. Fish. I was glad he was there, because it made possible my little joke: ours was the only church in town that employed Fish and Flipse! Upon returning to Westminster in 1963, we still had a Dutch custodian, Rinke Zielstra. Mrs. Zielstra helped a good deal, especially in Rinke’s last years when his health was failing. They were succeeded by Mr. and Mrs. Abe Thiessen, who in a year or two, became fed up with the sixty-eight steps to the fourth floor, and moved into their own home nearby. They also pointed out how very little private life they had by living in the building where they worked. In the church office, Mrs. Violet Ridd had been secretary for eight or ten years before our arrival. However, a few months into my tenure she suddenly resigned. Mrs. Anna Burt was hired to replace her, and for many years she served the church with great efficiency. She had been a Scottish war bride, coming to Canada shortly after the war to marry Phil whom she had met when they were both in the Armed Forces. They had two daughters, Margaret and Anne. Phil was an orderly at the Deer Lodge Veterans Hospital. Mrs. Burt was extremely efficient, and in short order had the office routine down to a science. She was with us till the late seventies, though her continuing problem with arthritis finally forced her to work from a wheelchair. They bought a van with a side-lift to assist in transporting her to the church until she reached retirement age. Her long-time dream was to tour Nova Scotia. (“I’ve toured all of old Scotland,” she used to say. “Now, if I could only see New Scotland.”) The church’s farewell gift to her was airfare for her and Phil to Halifax, with a para-transit van waiting for them to do a two-week tour in the Maritimes. Unfortunately, upon arrival back in Winnipeg, she suffered a bout of pneumonia, and after being bed-ridden for a few weeks, the arthritis became so severe she had to spend her remaining year and a half in bed. After her death, Phil began a life of travel, revisiting countries where he had served in WWII. He had been a very patient, caring, and long-serving husband.
