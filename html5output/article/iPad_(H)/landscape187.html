As the years moved along, I was conducting the funerals of elderly parishioners whom I had first known in the heyday of their working years. Two young lads whom I had confirmed graduated in law and were now “on the bench.” Others had moved on in one profession or another, taking an active part in the family   business, or establishing their own. As I said, it was somewhat frightening to return to Westminster, but it turned out to be a most challenging and fulfilling—and lengthy—pastorate. I mentioned earlier, in the section on Lakeview, that I recognized the demographic changes that were coming in that congregation. At Westminster, I had the opportunity to witness the changes that had already taken place. In the late 1940s, the change was beginning. Young people coming out of the Armed Forces were looking for property in the suburbs. The bulk of our congregation in those years still lived mostly in the Westminster-Fort Rouge-River Heights areas, with only relatively few as far out as Fort Garry or Deer Lodge. Subdivisions like Silver Heights, Kirkfield Park, and Windsor Park were only names. Oxford, Westworth, and Trinity Churches were nonexistent. Transcona was a separate town and we had only a smattering of members coming from what Larry Zolf calls “Winnipeg’s nort’end.” What a change we witnessed (and were part of) in our second tour of duty at Westminster! I remember well that day early in July 1963, as we drove into Winnipeg from the west: homes and stores and schools and churches right out to Kirkfield Park; there were apartment houses (albeit mostly three-storey walk-ups); Polo Park shopping mall already established at Portage and St. James; and hard by the St. James bridge overpass, the spanking new multi-storey Kiltarton Towers. Soon, we were to discover “Winnipeg’s finest” — the Edinburgh House on Wellington Crescent, and, as they say in almost every advertisement, “much, much more.” In a sense we were not fully prepared for the traumatic change at Westminster. Before we left Lakeview we had canvassed the possibility of having a seniors’ club, but there were so few members over sixty-five or seventy that such a club was considered neither necessary nor viable.
