The Club also erected a memorial window, dedicated on June, 1912 to W.J. Stafford. We still enjoy that beautiful window, located in the Narthex (entrance vestibule) of the Church. The picture depicts the story of David’s escape from the King who regarded him as competitor. The King’s son, Jonathon (with the bow and arrow) was a very close friend of David and betrayed his father to help his friend. The love these two young men had for each other is recounted in the First book of Samuel, Chapter 20. The Young Men’s Club held an annual picnic on July first in conjunction with the Young Ladies Club. The Men's prowess in sports is reported in an article on sports in this eBook. So they were an active and vibrant group during the ministries of both McKinnon and Christie. And then they faded and failed. Fast forward to 1928 and the beginning of the second Young Men's Club. This was at the tail end of the Christie years with the coming of John Sutherland Bonnell to take place in 1929. An article in 1937 by Club secretary, Leslie Hunt, starts with a flurry of news reports from September 1928: “Screeching from out of the Indies a tropical hurricane, like a giant sword unsheathed, raced over Florida, cutting down everything in its path and leaving behind 1000 dead and 700,000 homeless  … People in Winnipeg walked the streets in fear as schools remained closed, and the death toll from dread infantile paralysis slipped past the hundred mark … From far off China, came news of a terrible Moslem rebellion, with the massacres’ toll reaching 200,000 … Then Leslie gets down to work and switches to good news; the Club’s beginning. Two men in the congregation, James Crowe (see the article on our founders) and Stanley Jones (see the article on The Actimist Club) called together 12 young men on September 25th and proposed the formation of a Young Men’s Club. Two weeks later the Club was formed with Matt Tennent as President. Matt was soon transferred down East and was replaced by Eddie Grant. Leslie goes on to recount the Club’s activities: to the little settlement of Cormorant, on the Hudson Bay Railway went a bundle of Christmas cheer, gladdening the hearts of 22 children.
