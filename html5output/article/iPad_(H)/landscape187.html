I think of my own home (time period- 1930-1950) where mother was never apprised of how much money my father was earning. He gave her an allowance to run the house. No questions asked. No information shared. If she had the need to buy an article of clothing she had to ask father for the money. He would decide. This inbred belief that a woman couldn't make the big decisions was in both our micro and macro worlds. Then, sometime in the early 1950s someone (or someones) encouraged people in the United Church to recognize this male blindness. It took 9 years (1953 -62),  “painstaking study” and “wrestling with the problem” to turn this thinking around. it took another 8 years for Westminster to shuck off its Elders and Session and institute a General Board. With women. The most visible leader in the  move to create a General Board was Marilyn Huband. I could  suggest others, also, where instrumental on the process. But Marilyn became ground zero. In 1968 Mrs. C. R. Huband (Marilyn) was elected President of Winipeg Presbyterial (note the the last 4 letters of that word) of United Church Women,  and Mrs. Robert Jeske (Betty Jeske) was elected Treasurer. From those positions pressure was exerted to introduce the concept of Board at Westminster, which was realized in 1970 with Marilyn elected as the first Chair. A side note: In 1970 I , (R.H.),  was responsible for organizing the service of Communion  four times a year ( a task I had for ten years.). My job was to find 10 or 12 volunteers to be the servers each Communion.. By tradition the servers were always men. Shortly after the new Board took office Marilyn Huband phoned me: M: Roy, you are responsible for recruiting communion servers are you not?
