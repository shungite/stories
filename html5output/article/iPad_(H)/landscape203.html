Dorothy had made up the beds in our new home. And I set the alarm for 7:30 a.m. so I could return the rented trailer at 8 a.m. the next morning! It was small comfort to have the new owners of 141 come by that evening as we were frantically trying to finish our hectic day’s work to assure us that there really was no hurry after all. They had decided to have some redecoration done, and the painters weren’t coming for several days. Oh well, you can’t win ‘em all. I like to add footnotes, and this time there are two. First, don’t ever try to do your own moving, even if it is only five blocks away. And, second, we certainly didn’t realize at the time how extremely fortunate we were to have purchased our home when we did. We bought 718 Waterloo in 1971 for $23,900, and, exactly ten years later, sold it for $67,000. What a boon to have cash in hand to buy our retirement home in London, a much better home in every way, and almost completely paid for by the sale of 718 Waterloo. There were at least two important decisions regarding property and finances made by the trustees and board at Westminster in our time there. First, the old manse at 120 Maryland Street was being used by Manitoba conference as the office for the conference staff and the resource centre. (141 Waterloo had been bought as a manse in about 1958.) After fairly lengthy negotiations with the conference executive, Westminster agreed to tear down the old manse and build a two-storey office building to serve the conference needs. This was accomplished in 1964. The conference rent is used to pay off the $80,000 mortgage on the office building, so it has been a win-win situation for the church. The mortgage is just about paid off at this point (1992), and from now on, the conference rental will become church income for Westminster. Second, about 1965 I suggested that we should set up a foundation fund for the church. There were always people asking if there was some particular need toward which a memorial contribution could be made. Also, there were three or four small trust funds (not more than a total of $10,000–$15,000) and they were carried along year after year, reported at the annual meeting, but never used. No one quite knew what to do with them. It was my feeling that if there were a Foundation Fund properly set up and publicized, there would be contributions made to it.
