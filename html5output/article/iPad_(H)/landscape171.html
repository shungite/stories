This all was happening before Union, and much of it before women even had the vote. Churches were social centres, and, by looking at the parish histories of some churches, we can reconstruct how they filled this role. Clarence McKinnon, the minister of Westminster Church when it was located on Notre Dame and Hargrave, recalled: “Large after meetings were held on Sunday night. The social side of life was vigorously organized. Newcomers were met and welcomed. Picnics, socials followed each other in quick succession. Interchurch debates enlivened the winter months, and athletic sports in the summer. In my first year, a gentleman, a French Canadian, stopped me on the street to tell me how, when he and his wife were lonely on their first arrival in the city, they had strayed into Westminster Church on a Sunday evening and how grateful they were for the happy human fellowship they found there. (From Winnipeg’s Great War: A City Comes of Age by Jim Blanchard) Women became famous for hosting ‘tea-meetings’ or ‘soirees.’ Ads were often placed in the local newspaper, inviting the general public to join church members for good conversation and various programmes comprised of readings and music by the choir. Everyone was invited to indulge in a great variety of homemade delicacies — all for a nominal fee which went back to the Church coffers Reverend Christie, wrote of his first winter at the church in 1909 — “it was the hey day of immigration in Winnipeg. Sometimes rooming houses were not too attractive. To linger on the streets on the severe winter evenings was to invite frozen ears. If the church was a mecca for the faithful it was also a welcome refuge for the careless. Indeed on Sunday evenings the entire width of Hargrave Street was black with the stream of people, padding almost noiselessly over the snow, making their way to Knox or Central or First Baptist or Westminster. Groups could be seen, scurrying from one side to the other to these over crowded sanctuaries.” (From Winnipeg’s Great War: A City Comes of Age  by Jim Blanchard.)
