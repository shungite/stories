Italy, the country, was only 58 years old. Long before World War One the Italian government policy of irredentism (read ‘emancipation’) had been in practice. The country was adding — by force if necessary — islands and cities beyond their borders. Their pretext, flimsy but useful. was that Italian was spoken in these areas. By 1929 this policy of expansion had morphed into Fascism a form of radical authoritarian nationalism. Mussolini was not far behind. Communism, by an act of revolution, was established in Russia.  Its sister ideology Socialism was the choice of the French. The Lyons Congress (1878) advocated “emancipation of workers,” and “collective ownership of the soil’’ By the opening of the twentieth century France had clearly become a socialist country. Not Communist, but Socialist. (Clarence McKinnon, in his book Reminiscences , mentions this socialist development in France as “worrisome”). The Russian revolt against the Tzar spawned the Communist Party with it’s vain hope of peacefully changing the minds and activities of 163,000,000 Russians.The French Revolution successfully spawned Socialism and America’s War of Independence spawned what we know today as American- style Democracy. In Canada? Well, we had no need of a revolt. We had the Queen and we were busy building a country. And we didn’t have to go looking for an ideology. If you accept John Ralston Saul’s thesis in his book, A Fair Country, Canada has been deeply influenced and shaped by Aboriginal ideas and by the experience of both Francophone and Anglophone immigrants over the 250 years, from 1600 on, during which Aboriginals were either the dominant force in Canada, or equal partners. By 1929, there was a plethora of competing political ideologies: to Communism, Fascism, Socialism and Democracy we need to add German Nazism. The German Worker’s Party a pan-German nationalist and antisemitic party of which Hitler seized power in 1920 was the precursor of the National Socialist Party (NAZI Party). In 1933 the Nazi Party held the largest elected majority in the Reichstag and Hitler became Chancellor. In 1927 John Sutherland Bonnell, a man of considerable intelligence, seemingly boundless energy and a lover of ideas was in London, England, taking post-graduate studies — on leave from his church posting, St. Andrew’s Presbyterian in St. John, New Brunswick. He had been in Europe during WW I (twice wounded and a victim of a poison gas attack), and his interest in the continent was turning his mind in that direction.
