Were we being offered a “new role” for women in the hope it would stop the shrinking  in that half of our membership? Yes. To quote a UC document intended to create a positive spin on the plan, “All this must lead to a stronger and better church.” Rather it served as a distraction from something of which many of us in the United Church were not really aware. The Untied Church, like many Protestant denominations, wasn't (still isn't) ready to face the implications of a new world where overall change was happening at an accelerated rate.  And in some sectors, is now happening at a dizzying rate. But the United Church moves slowly; ever so slowly. At the beginning of the 20th century,  we note in an article elsewhere,  the concern in the Protestant churches ( especially the Presbyterian and Anglican denomination) that young men were deserting the church in their late teens. Not young women,. Young men. And yet, with this evident loss to our numbers and loss to our potential male  leadership we persisted for over 50 years in leaving the whole central control of the church in the hands of men. We didn't think to include women in the important areas of concern.We simply worked harder to keep young men involved. Sports, fellowship.More sports. I think of my own home (time period- 1930-1950) where mother was never apprised of how much money my father was earning. He gave her an allowance to run the house. No questions asked. No information shared. If she had the need to buy an article of clothing she had to ask father for the money. He would decide. This inbred belief that a woman couldn't make the big decisions was in both our micro and macro worlds. Then, sometime in the early 1950s someone (or someones) encouraged people in the United Church to recognize this male blindness. It took 9 years (1953 -62),  “painstaking study” and “wrestling with the problem” to turn this thinking around. it took another 8 years for Westminster to shuck off its Elders and Session and institute a General Board. With women. The most visible leader in the  move to create a General Board was Marilyn Huband. I could  suggest others, also, where instrumental on the process. But Marilyn became ground zero.
