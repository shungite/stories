In 1978, the United Church’s new Division of Mission in Canada (DMC) initiated a study into the UCWs across Canada who were struggling with the structure set in 1962 and with other issues. The study recommended that it would be beneficial for Conference presidents to meet regularly to discuss issues, concerns, and projects. The gathering was to be called a consultation, with no administrative or decision-making authority but Conference presidents continued to meet for many years. In 2000, at the General Council meeting, a resolution was passed stating that “all women’s groups within the Pastoral Charge or congregation are free to determine their size, structure, and manner of leadership.” At the 2003 General Council meeting, The Women’s Ministries Network was proposed and adopted. It would encompass the UCW, the only structured group, and all other women’s groups within the United Church. In 2003, the name National Consultation of United Church Women was changed to National United Church Women (NUCW). What we see today is women continuing to be strong contributors to the work of Westminster, but now, not so much as a separate entity, but full partners, on the board, on committees, as staff, as volunteers and as ministers. I found a write up done by Lorna Harvey on Yvonne Whitehead, done in 1984 that describes one woman’s role in the change. Yvonne transferred from Knox to Westminster in 1955. She joined Unit 13 and the J.S. Bonnell Mission. She was the last president of UCW before Westminster Church went to the General Board format. Prior to the end of her term she was on the committee which drew up the guide lines for the General Board. As chair of the Membership and Visiting Committee she reorganized the district visiting with the help of Gordon Pattison. She was Secretary of the Pastoral Relations Committee and a team Captain for the Stewardship Campaign. Marilyn Huband who was a leader, both in the Church and in the wider community, and was a friend to many of us here today. She brought energy, joy and insight to the needs of Westminster. No new visitor to the Church went un noticed by Marilyn — she made a point of welcoming all. And Marilyn started the Mother’s Morning Off, here at the Church to provide space and time for women from the area to come together while their children were attended. She knew it was very important to involve young women in the affairs of the Church. Another memory that Georgine Kerr sent me is of her mother Marguerite (Bonnie) Kerr who joined Westminster in 1936.
