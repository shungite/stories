Also, it has the power to act on behalf of the congregation. But there has always been a problem here. Why should we divide the spiritual and the temporal? Aren’t they all of a piece, really? Why should one particular group (the Stewards) do all the property work, and even more especially, all the financial decisions? Granted, all decisions are supposed to be checked by the Official Board, but this is usually only rubber-stamping. Also, it makes difficult communication within a church. Despite the Good Book to the contrary, the left hand should know what the right is doing! Another problem with the board structure at Westminster (common in many churches) was that there seemed to be no terminal point for many office holders. For example, when we went to Westminster in 1963, the same man had been clerk of Session for a dozen years or more and the same person had held the position as chairman of Stewards for ten years. Also, there were only three or four women on these boards. To discuss possible changes, we organized a four-hour session, which we called a Westminster forum. This may have been where the idea was born, viz., to have one church board as the governing body of our congregation. Out of that idea we decided to have three groups meet—the executives of the UCW, of Stewards, and of Session—together with staff. It is not possible to describe all that happened in that year of monthly meetings. But it soon became apparent how much overlapping there was: e.g., both the UCW and the stewards had finance and property committees; both the Session and the UCW had a mission committee and a flower committee, to give only a couple of examples. As well, there were times when people had to report to both Stewards and Session, and maybe, as well, to the Official Board. It was a clumsy and an inefficient type of organization. But, in setting up a new board structure, we had to make certain that all the work of the congregation would be covered, yet not duplicated. Also, we wanted to make certain that there would be a good mix of women, men, and young people on the board. We wanted to be certain, too, that there would be a time limit for any one person to serve.
