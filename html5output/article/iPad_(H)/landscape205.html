In the church office, Mrs. Violet Ridd had been secretary for eight or ten years before our arrival. However, a few months into my tenure she suddenly resigned. Mrs. Anna Burt was hired to replace her, and for many years she served the church with great efficiency. She had been a Scottish war bride, coming to Canada shortly after the war to marry Phil whom she had met when they were both in the Armed Forces. They had two daughters, Margaret and Anne. Phil was an orderly at the Deer Lodge Veterans Hospital. Mrs. Burt was extremely efficient, and in short order had the office routine down to a science. She was with us till the late seventies, though her continuing problem with arthritis finally forced her to work from a wheelchair. They bought a van with a side-lift to assist in transporting her to the church until she reached retirement age. Her long-time dream was to tour Nova Scotia. (“I’ve toured all of old Scotland,” she used to say. “Now, if I could only see New Scotland.”) The church’s farewell gift to her was airfare for her and Phil to Halifax, with a para-transit van waiting for them to do a two-week tour in the Maritimes. Unfortunately, upon arrival back in Winnipeg, she suffered a bout of pneumonia, and after being bed-ridden for a few weeks, the arthritis became so severe she had to spend her remaining year and a half in bed. After her death, Phil began a life of travel, revisiting countries where he had served in WWII. He had been a very patient, caring, and long-serving husband. Pat Hodges arrived in Winnipeg with her husband, Houston, just as the search committee was looking for a new church secretary. Houston was a United  Methodist minister from Texas, and had come to be the social action secretary of Winnipeg presbytery. Joan McConnell met the Hodges, discovered that Pat had been a church secretary in the deep south and was hoping to get employment in Winnipeg. It was a perfect fit, and for our last two years at Westminster, the phone was answered with a happy and cheery southern drawl. For many years, indeed, from its beginning in 1892, Westminster had a Session and a Deacon’s Court. The Deacon’s Court was something like an official board in that it consisted of all members of Session, plus people who were on the finance and property committee. There was no Committee of Stewards; this function was performed by the Deacon’s Court.
