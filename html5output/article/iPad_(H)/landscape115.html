The following piece describes Howse at war (1) with the Quebec Government and (2) with the Alberta Government and (3) with the Federal Government concerning other bad acts of legislation. In 1937 he preached sermons on “the extraordinary threat to democracy in two laws the passage of which had shocked Canadians: The Padlock Law in Quebec (officially called ‘Act to protect the Province Against Communistic Propaganda’) would give the Government the right to confiscate and “padlock” any house: The Act made it illegal to “use [a house or allow any person to make use of it to propagate Communism or Bolshevism by any means whatsoever” … The premier of the province could confiscate any books “tending to propagate Communism.” The Prime Minister, W.L.M. King could have asked Parliament to nullify the law but didn’t want to annoy Quebec voters who supported his Liberal Party. Another contentious act was Alberta’s ‘Accurate News Act’ enacted by William Aberhart’s Social Credit government. It would have required newspapers to print ‘clarifications’ of stories that a committee of Social Credit legislators deemed inaccurate, and to reveal their sources on demand. Both this and the Quebec laws were intended to control the Press in every word it could print. In the case of Alberta, the Lieutenant-Governor of Alberta refused Royal Assent until the Supreme Court evaluated the law. Assent was denied. During the War a number of Canadian Communists were jailed for opposing our War effort. They pleaded for permission to go to Russia and fight against the Nazis. Howse, who spoke at a ‘Citizens’ Rally’ at the Walker Theatre (Burton Cummings Theatre now) sponsored by the Communist Party took the position — If they want to go the Russia and fight in the war, let them go! In his speech, he also advocated the removal of the war-time ban on the Communist Party. The Government’s War Measures Act was also, for Howse, an abomination. The Act was questioned for its suspension of civil liberties and personal freedoms, not only for Ukrainians and other Europeans during Canada's first national internment operations of 1914–1920, but also during the Second World War’s Japanese Canadian internment.
