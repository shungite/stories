The Long Road To Ministry Ernest’s academic road to ministry started in Toronto. He attempted to register at Victoria College. Their Honours courses in Philosophy, English and History were, he was told, an excellent foundation for his further studies. However the College required him to have background in Latin- he had none- and more Mathematics and Science than he had taken in St.John’s. On the other hand,, they said, steering him away from Victoria, Albert College in Belleville ,Ontario had an excellent program in tutoring students who needed to take more than the regular curriculum. Hence, his first year of university at Albert College included a full set of first year courses plus Latin, Mathematics and Science. He excelled. “Of course I had to work hard. But I had a room to myself and I made it a practice to get up at 5:00 AM, wrap myself up in a carriage blanket ( the rooms were dreadfully cold early morning in winter) and get in uninterrupted hours before breakfast.” He excelled in his studies and also spent weekends preaching in a number of churches in that area of old Ontario. He also belonged to the debating society and joined the college’s rugby team (he lacked weight but, as he said, he was a very fast runner!). He then needed that summer to earn money to continue his studies, he assumed, at Victoria College. But jobs were few and pay was minimal. At the end of the summer he still didn’t have the means to register for university courses. This meant a year of earning before more learning. Despite the scarcity of jobs he managed to dig sewers with a pick and shovel, and clerk at Eaton’s over Christmas. In the new year, despairing of finding anything worth while he applied to the Church’s Superintendent of Home Missions for a job as a student minister in western Canada. His application was accepted and he was placed in Forget, Saskatchewan, a home mission field in southern Saskatchewan with three points; Forget, Ossa and Coteau. When he reached his mission he found that travel was a problem and there had been no provision for it. There were no roads, only concession trails dividing up the land. He needed a horse, which to his surprise was then loaned to him by a local rancher. He boarded for a time in Forget and his horse, Ruby, ably carried him to his three congregations.
