Although, of course, no church is complete without its clubs, sub-committees, and cliques. Over 1893-94, the Ladies Society, Young Ladies Mission Band, Westminster Auxiliary of the Women’s Foreign Missionary Society, and Junior Young Peoples Society of Christian Endeavour were all formed. And what about a seating plan? The tradition in the Presbyterian Church used to be that pews were allocated to families and rent was charged accordingly. Westminster decided to continue the allocation process but opted however not to charge its congregants rent, eliminating one of its only two sources of revenue. Generous though this gesture was, effectively opening up church membership to Presbyterians of lesser means, it carried an implied injunction: keep that collection plate flowing with donations! Although moribund today, this tradition — we mean of seat allocation; church’s solicitation of charity is alive and well — can still be seen in our own pews, which are fixed with a numbered metal plate at the end of each row. The practice of assigning families to specific numbered pews was in effect until 1919 when the size of the congregation made this arrangement impractical. This growth was a reflection of a couple factors. By 1893, the population of Winnipeg was slightly under 34,000. The Winnipeg Electric Railway had ten streetcars (which didn’t operate on Sundays, thank you). There were no paved sidewalks and no asphalt roads.
