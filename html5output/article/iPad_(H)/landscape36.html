Although moribund today, this tradition — we mean of seat allocation; church’s solicitation of charity is alive and well — can still be seen in our own pews, which are fixed with a numbered metal plate at the end of each row. The practice of assigning families to specific numbered pews was in effect until 1919 when the size of the congregation made this arrangement impractical. This growth was a reflection of a couple factors. By 1893, the population of Winnipeg was slightly under 34,000. The Winnipeg Electric Railway had ten streetcars (which didn’t operate on Sundays, thank you). There were no paved sidewalks and no asphalt roads. There were very few buildings of any sort west of Sherbrooke Street. In other words, the Wolseley community as we know it didn’t exist. Yet by the time the Westminster congregation looked for new quarters in 1910, 17 years later, the logical place to go was the burgeoning area where many of Westminster’s people were now already living. By moving to Wolseley, Westminster would have also attracted to its congregation other residents of the Gates, Crescentwood, and Wolseley wishing to take advantage of their proximity to the newly erected church. § Back to the Hargrave days of Westminster. In 1899, there were 262 congregation members and counting; much of this growth being due to the popularity of Westminster’s evening services. Early in February, Reverend Pitblado was granted a six month-leave of absence — ill health again. He and Mrs. Pitblado embarked on what the family records and our own call “a trip around the world.” The congregation and a large portion of the general public were treated to letters back from the Pitblados as the tour unfolded. We have an interesting “news” item from a Hawaiian publication, the Hawaiian Weekly : “The Rev. C.B. Pitblado, pastor of one of the leading Presbyterian Churches in Winnipeg, Canada now in Honolulu on his return from India, China and Japan, having visited the missions of his own denomination, especially in India, and having made careful inquiries into the work of missionaries, bears testimony of the great improvement in sentiments touching Christianity. In towns and villages where a few years ago the Evangelists were refused admission, they are now welcomed
