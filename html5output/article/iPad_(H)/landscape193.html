Reid writes: We so thoroughly enjoyed the British television version of Brideshead Revisited , that I thought the heading ‘Westminster Revisited’ was at least somewhat appropriate for this chapter. We, too, found ourselves reliving our earlier years, 1946–1949, often reminiscing with parishioners about the glory days back in the forties when the church was packed every Sunday morning; when an average Sunday evening service had an attendance of three or four hundred; when there were between 100 and 150 in the Apex Young People’s group; when I organized the junior youth group (mostly Grade 11 and 12 young people) whose innovative programs were among the most imaginative I have ever seen. I’ll never forget the evening they conducted a funeral for “the church” — complete with a casket (borrowed from Mordue’s Funeral Home). After a very impressive eulogy in which the church’s earlier virtues were extolled, the casket was opened and those attending were invited to “please come forward now to view the deceased.” How surprised we were when we looked in the silk-lined casket to see ourselves, reflected in a mirror that had been appropriately placed at just the right angle in the bottom of the casket! To return as senior minister after fourteen years was both an interesting and overwhelming (if not frightening) experience. As the young assistant (I was only thirty-two when we first went to Westminster in 1946), I had been given quite wide-ranging responsibilities. Dr. Howse was an excellent senior minister, and one never had the feeling of simply being the assistant to the minister. I, too, was a minister, and he had the happy faculty of inspiring confidence in me by the way in which he left whole areas of work for me to do. He was always available when I wanted (or needed) to consult with him, but I never felt that he was peeking around the corner to see what I was doing. I had work to do, and he left me to it: the overall Christian education program, Sunday and mid-week, work with new members and confirmation classes, home and hospital visiting, and a limited amount of preaching, except in the summer when I preached morning and evening from the last Sunday in June to Labour Day Sunday. In the summer, of course, I was in charge of all the work of the church— Sunday services, weddings, funerals, hospital visiting — the whole bit.
