The units were still meeting, but they came under the fellowship committee. There were still “districts” and “district visiting,” but the visitation was arranged by the membership and visiting committee. And, best of all, we were finding new people every year to become board members, and all the board members were aware of all the work in the church. In short, we became a much more open society. And with this new breath of fresh air blowing in the church, it gave me new impetus, and was the chief reason, I think, why I found it easy to continue on at Westminster for a few more years, and eventually till retirement when I reached age sixty-five in 1979. There were changes in our household, too. Doug, who had enrolled in Grade 9 at River Heights Junior High when we came to Winnipeg in 1963, went on to Kelvin High and then to do his BA at the University of Winnipeg, graduating with the gold medal for highest standing in the general arts course. He had worked each summer (on the end of a hoe) at the University of Manitoba farm, and had a bank account to go to York University in Toronto in the fall of 1970 to pursue a PhD in psychology. So, in our huge manse at 141 Waterloo Street (3,200 square feet of floor space and a 100 × 150 foot property), we were down to one family member at home. Rob had proceeded through elementary, junior high, and was now (1970–1971) in his final year at Kelvin High School and had made his decision to go east for his university work. We certainly didn’t need this huge manse. Dorothy was already on part-time staff at Laura Second as teacher-librarian, having taken the necessary five courses at U of M. A smaller home would suit us better. It would also give us opportunity to get some equity in a house, so that upon retirement we wouldn’t simply leave the keys on the kitchen counter and face the prospect of having to buy a home at that late stage. I spoke to some of the people on the board who had been around for years about buying our own house, although I assured them that if the congregation felt strongly about retaining its manse we could no doubt accommodate ourselves to their decision.
