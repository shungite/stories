In 1916 the family made its next move across the Bonaventure Bay to Wesleyville.  After three years living there Ernest completed as much education as he could get in an Outport School and headed off to the Methodist College in St. John’s as his father and his older sisters had. For one last reference to Outport life I look at Ernest’s last year at home before setting off for college. During the summer, to earn money to help pay his tuition he joined a neighbour of the family named Bill. Bill was a fisherman who enjoyed “handlining.” This technique employed a line outfitted with a single hook baited with clams and such. Familiar to most of us. But handlining could sometime turn into “jigging.” This variation had two unbated hooks and a lead sinker, shaped as a fish at the end. The lead would pull the line to the bottom. Then the line would be pulled up slightly and jigged up and down to snag passing cod. The money Ernest earned that last summer was paid, by Bill, to his parents and salted away for the Methodist College tuition. By the end of his 16th summer he said goodbye to his family and brought to a close an epoch in his life. On he went, 16 years of age, to St. John’s and the world. Leaving Home For Schooling “Many times before that morning on the steamer (sailing into St.Johns harbour) I had been given to puzzled meditation about the strangeness of our existence, the mystery of our own little being in the infinity of the universe.” Ernest was conscious of the small settlements in which he had spent the entirety of his life. On the one hand he recognized how little they had and yet, as he says several times in his autobiography, he never felt “poor”. Then, too, there was the immense sea, and men working their small boats to wrest a living from it. And the women who waited at home; waiting and waiting; lives tenuous and vulnerable.
