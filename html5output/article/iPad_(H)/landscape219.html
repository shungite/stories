What a night! During the supper hour, members of the planning committee took Dorothy and me to every table, stopping just long enough to say hello. It was a thrill to be able to call practically every individual person by name—one advantage of a long pastorate. The next day we went down to Encore Travel and booked a three-week tour to Greece for September. It was a great finale to a fabulous sixteen years—actually sixteen years plus the three years as assistant. There were only two more weeks to go, and the days and nights were filled with luncheons and dinner parties hosted by so many kind folk. We completed plans to rent our house to Dr. Brian Bevan, a middle-aged English doctor who was soon to bring his bride to Canada, and they were happy to rent a completely furnished house for nine months while they decided where they might wish to build or buy. We spent a few days packing all our clothes from clothes closets and bedroom drawers, plus all my books and files from the vestry—a grand total of thirty boxes to be stored in the furnace room! Everything else was left “as is”—bedding, dishes, pictures on the walls, etc. At $375 per month, we think the Bevans had a good deal. We trusted that they would look after things well, and we found out when we came home on April 30, 1980, that everything was in good order. The pulpit supply committee had arranged for Sunday services for July 1979, but since we were in town, I had offered to take funerals and weddings, and it turned out to be one of those very busy months. But I was not about to leave people scrambling to find someone to conduct a funeral, or to have a stranger for their wedding when we were in the city anyway. So it worked out okay. As an appendix to this chapter I shall include my second-last sermon at Westminster, which I entitled, “The First Forty Years are the Hardest.” There will not be much overlapping with what has been written thus far, but perhaps it gives a kind of summary. (Let me confess here that ten years later, in 1989, when I was completing my second post-retirement job, at Metropolitan in London, I used almost exactly the same sermon, simply changing the title to “The First Fifty Years are the Hardest.”)
