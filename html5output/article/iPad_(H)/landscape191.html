Also, we wanted to make certain that there would be a good mix of women, men, and young people on the board. We wanted to be certain, too, that there would be a time limit for any one person to serve. We began the discussion and study of a possible new structure of congregational government at the beginning of 1969, and the final report was presented to the congregational meeting in January 1970. It was approved. The General Board (as we decided to call it) would be made up of nine committees, with four persons on each committee—worship, education, finance, property, communications, membership and visiting, fellowship, outreach, and nominations. There would also be a chairperson, vice chair, secretary, and treasurer, with the staff as ex officio members. Each board member would serve a four-year term, and the chair would change every second year. Each of the nine committees could add to their numbers for their own committee tasks, but all major decisions would be brought to the monthly meeting of the General Board for final approval. This obviously is a very brief description of a very major change in congregational government. (The text of the constitution is in my Westminster file.) I have included this summary of our church board restructuring because it changed in a major way the focus of my work at Westminster. It gave a whole new direction to the life and work of the congregation. I was really impressed with the way in which a structure, or system of government, can change the life of a church. For many years, the congregation seemed to be pulled in three directions by the Session, by the UCW, and by the Stewards. Now, there was only one board. There were no financial secrets tucked away in the Stewards’ safekeeping. There was no Session, so any members of the congregation could be, and were, asked to serve the elements on Communion Sunday. The UCW had no secret agenda, because there was no UCW! The units were still meeting, but they came under the fellowship committee. There were still “districts” and “district visiting,” but the visitation was arranged by the membership and visiting committee. And, best of all, we were finding new people every year to become board members, and all the board members were aware of all the work in the church. In short, we became a much more open society.
