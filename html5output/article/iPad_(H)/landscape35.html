3. THE BUILDING •   April 19th 1894: A committee strikes to consider the cost of a new church building •   May 3rd: Architect George Browne is selected and engaged to prepare plans •   May 4th: Pitblado family arrives in Winnipeg •   May 10th: Mr. James Scott, real estate dealer, appointed to find property on which to build •   May 31st: A site on the southeast corner of Notre Dame and Charlotte (Hargrave)     is purchased at a cost of $3750 •   July 26th: Contractors are engaged, and construction starts immediately •   December 31st: The first service is conducted in the new basement structure •   January 16th 1894: The first annual congregational meeting is held •   July 31st: Westminster’s structure is completed Having acquired a fine new home — which cost about $40,000, between furnishings, construction, and site acquisition — and a pastor, Westminster United could now properly call itself a church. Although, of course, no church is complete without its clubs, sub-committees, and cliques. Over 1893-94, the Ladies Society, Young Ladies Mission Band, Westminster Auxiliary of the Women’s Foreign Missionary Society, and Junior Young Peoples Society of Christian Endeavour were all formed. And what about a seating plan? The tradition in the Presbyterian Church used to be that pews were allocated to families and rent was charged accordingly. Westminster decided to continue the allocation process but opted however not to charge its congregants rent, eliminating one of its only two sources of revenue. Generous though this gesture was, effectively opening up church membership to Presbyterians of lesser means, it carried an implied injunction: keep that collection plate flowing with donations!
Rev. Charles Bruce Pitblado
