We hired movers, of course, to move the piano, living room and bedroom furniture and appliances, but did the rest ourselves. And we did it all in one day, June 30, 1971. The people from whom we bought didn’t move out till June 29, and the people who bought 141 wanted to move in on July 1. With a rented two-wheel trailer, and the help of some of Rob’s friends with a station wagon, we made an untold number of trips up and down Waterloo Street. Loads of books, the bricks and boards from our homemade shelves, all our dishes (packed by Dorothy), all our clothes still on hangers, bedding, pictures off the walls, plants in their pots, groceries from the shelves, lawn mower and wheel barrow and stepladder... and the list goes on. We took the last trailer load at midnight. Dorothy had made up the beds in our new home. And I set the alarm for 7:30 a.m. so I could return the rented trailer at 8 a.m. the next morning! It was small comfort to have the new owners of 141 come by that evening as we were frantically trying to finish our hectic day’s work to assure us that there really was no hurry after all. They had decided to have some redecoration done, and the painters weren’t coming for several days. Oh well, you can’t win ‘em all. I like to add footnotes, and this time there are two. First, don’t ever try to do your own moving, even if it is only five blocks away. And, second, we certainly didn’t realize at the time how extremely fortunate we were to have purchased our home when we did. We bought 718 Waterloo in 1971 for $23,900, and, exactly ten years later, sold it for $67,000. What a boon to have cash in hand to buy our retirement home in London, a much better home in every way, and almost completely paid for by the sale of 718 Waterloo. There were at least two important decisions regarding property and finances made by the trustees and board at Westminster in our time there. First, the old manse at 120 Maryland Street was being used by Manitoba conference as the office for the conference staff and the resource centre. (141 Waterloo had been bought as a manse in about 1958.) After fairly lengthy negotiations with the conference executive, Westminster agreed to tear down the old manse and build a two-storey office building to serve the conference needs.
