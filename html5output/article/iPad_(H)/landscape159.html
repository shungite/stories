Westminster Church … After an anxious period of waiting ( for this was the most important event in the applicant’s musical life so far), there came one day a call to try out … How often he had passed by that magnificent facade of Westminster Church, and dreamed that, somehow, one day, he might perhaps realize the secret, but nevertheless very real ambition that he might become the organist …” His official appointment as organist was made in May, 1920. By the mid-1920s Herb realized the need to complete his organ training. He thereafter studied, first under Hugh Ross and later under Douglas Clarke and Hugh Bancroft. He ultimately received both his ACCO ( Associate Canadian College of Organists) and FTCL (Fellow Trinity College London.) diplomas. He then became not only our man but a major ‘player’ in the musical life of Winnipeg. He was conductor of The Winnipeg Male Voice Choir-1935 to 1944, the Winnipeg Philharmonic Choir (with Filmer Hubble) from 1936 to 1945, and the 85-voice Metropolitan Choir (circa 1939-45) where, among other repertoire, he conducted Arthur Sullivan’s cantata The Golden Legend . A form of musical theatre was also to his liking with the Metropolitan Choir. They performed Scenes from the Song of Hiawatha  — Herb was the composer. The review in the Free Press  was not entirely kind: “The performance … held the kind of mistakes which commonly belong on opening night by amateurs, and possibly there were more than usual. One felt sometimes that the chorus as well as the principals could have done better by the composer  … The tenor for Onaway! Awake Beloved ! had to keep turning his eyes down to a copy of the words (unsuccessfully hidden) … The solo part for Hiawatha was rather sentimentalized; the singer did not quite know his Indians … One of the trees was observed to turn over steadily the pages of what might have been a musical score … One or two of the principals seemed to wander on and off the stage aimlessly now and then … The orchestra of twenty odd players showed themselves to be in fair sympathy with the singers …” Oscar Stauss’s comic opera, The Chocolate Soldier , Fraser Simson’s The Maid of the Mountains in 1945, and Smetana’s opera The Bartered Bride . were other theatre pieces essayed by Herb and the Metropolitan Choir with uneven success. The review of The Bartered Bride  called it a “Sincere Effort.” Some would call that Damned with Faint Praise!
