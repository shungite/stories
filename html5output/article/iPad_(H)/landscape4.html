Stories of Westminster United Church & its People / Page 2
The back hall of our church has always been a kind of picture gallery. There, we find a smattering of old pictures of people identified as our church ‘Founders,’ some with undecipherable signatures ,and a row of larger pictures, many in colour, of our ‘Ministers.’ We used to also find another row, now removed, of dignified but cloudily photographed faces of our ‘Assistant Ministers.’ I have always understood this collection as a kind of homage to those who have nurtured us, led us. But, for me, this gallery says more about the people who hung these pictures than their subjects. The ‘Founders’ section, for instance, is a rather puny sample of the 55 people recorded as composing the adventurers who struck out to create a new church. Why were these particular people chosen to represent the larger whole? Why is the church’s first Minister’s wife identified as a church founder, when in fact she and her husband were not among those who spearheaded its establishment? Another somewhat subtle but telling detail: the differences in size among these profiles, seeming to designate some men — and they are always men — as more noteworthy than others. The big men and the little men. And no women. Where are the church’s women Assistant Ministers, Deaconesses, and Christian Education Directors? Today, we are more uneasy with such sexist omissions, as, I hope, we are more disposed to scrutinize the other elitisms that can influence the layout of something so mundane, but representative, as a church’s hall of notables. One clear question, guiding my research and the spirit of this book, emerged from such scattered thoughts: who, after all, were and are the real notables of the church, officially recognized as such or otherwise? If on setting out on this project, I thought I could authoritatively answer this question, my research soon disabused me of such a conceit. It all began, as these things so often do, in an unassuming fashion. An assistant minster reminded me that our church’s 125th anniversary was approaching, and a book would probably be published to acknowledge the event. Why didn’t I contribute a single article to that book, he asked? I dutifully did so, discussing major themes in Minister Bonnell’s theological thinking as captured in his sermons at Westminster and at Fifth Avenue Presbyterian in New York. Before I knew it, I was researching to write a biography of Bonnell and Westminster’s other three founding ministers: Pitblado, MacKinnon, Christie and Bonnell.
