But Charles was “no common man … The whole congregation listen to his words with unmoved interest.” (The author’s use of the word ‘unmoved’ here seems equivocal, perhaps deliberately; we’re unsure if Homespun means to say that the congregation was left cold by Charles’ preaching, or rather that it inspired their unwavering attention.) Homespun continues, “His discourses often rise to flights of eloquence worthy of the foremost preachers of the day. It’s little short of a miracle that the people of Chalmers Church can retain his services as their pastor.” In other words Chalmers had more than they rightly deserved in Reverend Pitblado. We hope they felt good about that. In 1879/80 during a four-month leave from Chalmers, Charles travelled to Winnipeg, meeting colleague Reverend Professor Bryce. The pair then moved south to Emerson and finally west to obtain a knowledge of North Western missions. In the same article just mentioned, Homespun questioned whether his leave was really a professional necessity or simply an excuse for a holiday away from his family. (Although it doesn’t appear, in other regards, that Homespun was very well informed about the subject of his article; so perhaps we should take his wry observations with a grain of salt.) As a practicing minister in the early 1870s, Charles’ had served as Chair of the Committee on Education in the Presbyterian Synod of the Maritime provinces. Eventually, he was named a convenor (an officer) of the Manitoba Presbyterian College, incorporated March 1875. We don’t know the specifics of how A led to B; although we suspect that through the Presbyterian Church’s grapevine the relevant Manitobans were alerted to his demonstrated interest in education and eminence in Halifax. Whatever Charles’ reason for coming west, he was recruited by Manitoba Presbytery to engage in that fact-finding mission to the Prince Albert Mission. He and Bryce men travelled by buckboard (picture below), an uncomfortable ride, all the way to Prince Albert (a mission established by the Presbyterian Church to the Cree Indians). On the trip, Pitblado kept a diary from which he sent back several accounts of the journey. A brief quotation from the first diary entry: “Wednesday, 25th August, five o’clock.
