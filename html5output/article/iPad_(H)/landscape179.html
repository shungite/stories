So they were an active and vibrant group during the ministries of both McKinnon and Christie. And then they faded and failed. Fast forward to 1928 and the beginning of the second Young Men's Club. This was at the tail end of the Christie years with the coming of John Sutherland Bonnell to take place in 1929. An article in 1937 by Club secretary, Leslie Hunt, starts with a flurry of news reports from September 1928: “Screeching from out of the Indies a tropical hurricane, like a giant sword unsheathed, raced over Florida, cutting down everything in its path and leaving behind 1000 dead and 700,000 homeless  … People in Winnipeg walked the streets in fear as schools remained closed, and the death toll from dread infantile paralysis slipped past the hundred mark … From far off China, came news of a terrible Moslem rebellion, with the massacres’ toll reaching 200,000 … Then Leslie gets down to work and switches to good news; the Club’s beginning. Two men in the congregation, James Crowe (see the article on our founders) and Stanley Jones (see the article on The Actimist Club) called together 12 young men on September 25th and proposed the formation of a Young Men’s Club. Two weeks later the Club was formed with Matt Tennent as President. Matt was soon transferred down East and was replaced by Eddie Grant. Leslie goes on to recount the Club’s activities: to the little settlement of Cormorant, on the Hudson Bay Railway went a bundle of Christmas cheer, gladdening the hearts of 22 children. From the perspective of the 21st Century we picture a group of children spread out on the floor stone drunk from all that “Christmas cheer.” During the 4th season from the public platform over 50 addresses (speeeches) were given to outside organizations and a program of service was launched to country churches. The club also held an ‘undenominational’ mass meeting in April to 4000 people. In 1933 the club, with the help of the YMCA organized “two memorable mass meetings, the principal speaker being Dr. Sherwood Eddy. (This event is mentioned in the article on Dr. Bonnell.) Leslie indicates that over 6000 people attended. Here, the writer has to recognize that Leslie Hunt was strong on hyperbole but short on simple honest mathematics.
