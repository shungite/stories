It was 1885. The growth of Winnipeg’s population and commercial sector was accelerating at an incredible pace, which it would maintain for the next several decades. An industrial force was transforming this small, frozen, and isolated town of 2000 people — made up primarily of Aboriginals, Métis, Scot, English and French, and latched to a Hudson’s Bay Company fur trading post called Fort Garry — into a bustling hub, a ‘Gateway To The West.’ That force was, of course, the railway — more specifically the transcontinental railway, whose “last spike” was driven into the ground that same year. Such changes were greeted by Winnipeg’s diverse population with excitement and anger, malaise and optimism; the rapid modernization of this prairie town would have its winners and losers, divided along the predictable fault lines of race and class. First, there was the laying of thousands of miles of tracks over land that had from the beginning of time been open and seemingly boundless — the home of Blackfoot, Cree, Ojibwa, and other First Nations, along with their Métis cousins. Surveyors parceled the natives’ land into plots for homesteading; land that nourished the natives’ spiritual beliefs and supported their livelihood. They had tried to negotiate with the white man’s government located far to the East — and were pointedly ignored. Then came the steam-belching locomotives that pulled strings of carriages holding thousands of people who would also come to depend for their lives on the land. Their main wish was to work, farm, raise crops and families. Some were from Upper Canada — Ontario — but many more, thousands even, were streaming in from across at the Atlantic. They, too, had lost their land, many driven from homes they had know for generations due to religious persecution, famine, and unendurable poverty. But what did they know or care about the people they were displacing, and the devastating effects this displacement would have on the natives’ traditional economies and culture? How much did the average new settler know of the treaties, and their unfulfilled promises to provide the Aboriginals with the tools — fertile land, farm animals, and equipment — to transition to an agricultural way of life, and would it have troubled their conscience?
