David Christie, Our Third Minister —from North Shields to Westminster When I set out to write this article, the person, David Christie, was largely unknown to me or to most members of our present congregation. However, through serendipity and some personal research digging a picture emerged of a man, a World and a congregation all dealing with incredible change throughout his nearly 20 years as our minister. 1909-1910 For Westminster Church, 1909-1910 was a period for key decision-making. Firstly, Clarence MacKinnon had left us in 1909 and we had looked to Glasgow, Scotland for our third minister. Secondly, we were reaching out to the community at large. Members of our congregation (Sidney Smith, John Bellingham and their wives) were busy establishing an independent Sunday School on Ellice Avenue at Beverley Street. This was at MacKinnon’s urging. He felt that children in the “West End” (Ellis Ave., Sergeant, Notre Dame) had nowhere to turn to learn about Jesus (Clarence’s words). The Ellis-at-Beverly Sunday School would move into an old Anglican church building (we now call it the West End Cultural Centre) and would eventually move again to the old St. Stephan’s Presbyterian building on Portage Avenue to become Elim Chapel. (For the record the St. Stephen’s building became available to Smith and Bellingham when the St. Stephan’s congregation abandoned it in 1924 to join with the Broadway Methodist Congregation to become the St. Stephan’s-Broadway United Church in the Broadway Methodist site.) MacKinnon also recommended the establishment at another site for a Sunday School at 69 Furby Street South. This group would join us later at our new building on Maryland Street. Thirdly we had outgrown our building at the corner of Notre Dame and Charlotte (Hargrave). The building which was designed to hold up to nearly 1000 was already handling a congregation of 940 people which showed every sign of reaching further than that. Compare that figure with our opening figure in 1892 of 200 people.
