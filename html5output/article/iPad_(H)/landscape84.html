There’s no doubt that the congregation of Westminster Church cherished him as a wonderful preacher. The proof, of course was that he filled our sanctuary most Sundays to overflowing. With a sanctuary seating capacity of 1100 at times the overflow had to be accommodated in our second floor auditorium and first floor lecture hall seating perhaps another couple of hundred. The titles or his sermonizing while at Westminster reflect the ideas and concerns close to his heart. The examples of his writing in the United States reflect the same concerns in Theology and World Politics that we would have heard in our own sanctuary. What follows is an example of Bonnell Oratory that clearly demonstrates his unerring choice of vocabulary, his clever use of drama and a remarkable disdain for accuracy. Wendell Wilke died. He had been a prominent American lawyer who challenged Franklin Roosevelt for the office of President in 1940. A Democrat-turned-Republican he lost to Democrat Roosevelt. But Wilkie was a man or flexible ideals who remade himself to be so politically acceptable to Roosevelt (ie remade himself as a Democrat) that he became the President’s travelling spokesman, an informal ambassador—at—large. After his one defeat he never succeeded in being elected to anything. But as an exceptionally prominent person he fuelled Bonne||’s imagination. Wilkie’s funeral was held in Bonnell’s church. The opening paragraph of the Minister’s Eulogy reads as follows: “Seldom have the American people been so shocked and stunned as when tidings flashed across this nation and around the world that Wendell Wilkie had died. Men and women discussed the news in hushed tones and with awed voices. In homes of every class, and race and creed there was something more than a realization of national deprivation. There was a poignant sense of personal bereavement.” Bonnell flies even higher in the rest of the piece.
