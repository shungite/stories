According to Charles’ final report to Manitoba Presbytery he travelled over 800 miles to reach Prince Albert Mission. Notwithstanding his tribulations in getting there, he was impressed with the scene he encountered at his destination: “The school at the Mission under the charge of Miss Lucy Margaret Baker is doing very efficient work. Through it a large number of children who speak the Cree language are receiving a good education”. We trust that Minnie Campbell would have likewise approved of this “good education”; in all likelihood it contained a mixture of “moral”, English, and rudimentary technical lessons designed to assimilate its students into the quickly modernizing, Anglocentric society of Canada. Upon his return to Winnipeg, Charles was given $150 “on behalf of his expenses” which he returned to the Presbytery’s Home Mission fund to finance mission work in the “North West” (i.e. land that we now call Saskatchewan). For his donation he received the cordial thanks of the Presbytery. Thus concluded Charles’ “vacation” to the frontier, and he returned to his congregation at Chalmers Presbyterian in Halifax. But he would be back to Manitoba after not long, albeit in less rugged circumstances. A year later, in 1881, he received a call from a new Winnipeg congregation that had recently been born out of Knox Presbyterian – located in those days at Ellice and Donald. Aware of his good ministerial work in Halifax and as a missionary in Manitoba, they wanted him to lead their church. A bit about that church: the congregation called themselves St. Andrew’s Elgin Avenue Church (as the picture shows) and their temple was located at the corner of Elgin and Ellen north of Notre Dame Avenue. As mentioned earlier, this congregation was soon to give birth to two more churches: Westminster in 1892 and St. Andrews-River Heights in 1915. This “schism” was due not to friction between congregants but rather to the exceptional growth of the city in the late 1800s and early 1900s. In 1874, Winnipeg’s population was roughly 2000. By the last decade of that century the number stood just under 34,000. The city’s churches found themselves growing in congregant numbers beyond the capacity of their buildings, and as new neighbourhoods opened, church members were often enticed to move to dwellings further afield from their home churches.
