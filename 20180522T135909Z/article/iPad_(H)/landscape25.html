The story goes that James was in charge of the auger they were using for their drilling. Each time the auger was raised it was diligently examined (in the mud encrustation) for any signs of the precious metal. One day, the story continues, James raised the auger, saw a piece of something in the mud, and quickly grabbed it and shoved it in his pocket. In doing this, he was seen by one of the groups’ partners. He was questioned and he promised to show his find at the groups’ next meeting — but never relinquished it for examination. At this point the story has several different ending depending on who tells it. One is that James simply returned to Scotland; another that James moved to the U.S. (possibly to the New England states); yet another that James quickly left the island and died shortly thereafter. (But, alas, God did not strike him down for his duplicity: we know for a fact that James died in 1903 at the age of 81.) For some writers the whole Pitblado incident — that the brothers “salted: their dig in The Money Pit”  — was bogus. Nevertheless, digging continued after the Pitblado incident and the possibility of pirate gold still inspires people to dig there even to this day. And talk of the Pitblado brothers’ involvement with Oak Island Gold can still be found on the Internet as part of the larger story. We are told that Charles Bruce was a young man of uncommon intelligence, and that his father, John, valued education and supported his son’s studies. During that period of the boy’s study at the Normal School in Truro, John was employed in the construction of the Nova Scotia railway from Halifax to Pictou. Charles Bruce worked with his father in the summers, earning money for his winter studies. Unfortunately, John was killed in an explosion at Mount Uniake in 1857. This left Charles Bruce, age 19, as the oldest son among ten siblings. Being the only family member with on-the-job experience with the railway, he temporarily left education to complete his father’s contract until a younger brother was old enough to take his place. After returning to his studies, Pitblado first was induced by Mr. Adams G. Archibald (afterwards Lieutenant Governor of Manitoba) to enter his law office. While thus engaged he decided that law wasn’t the right profession for him, and he turned to studies related to church ministry; first at The Free Church College in Halifax, and then at Presbyterian Seminary in Truro. He completed his studied in 1865, becoming Reverend Charles Bruce Pitblado.
