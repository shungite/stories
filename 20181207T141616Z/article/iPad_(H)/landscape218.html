Both the national church boards and the cross-border ecumenical programs were interesting and, I think, worthwhile. The interfaith contacts were also very valuable, especially to meet monthly or so with clergy, priests, and rabbis in Winnipeg, and to have occasional joint services in synagogues and cathedrals. Who was it once said, “I don’t like that person,” and his friend replied, “But you don’t know him.” “No,” was the response, “and that’s why I don’t like him.” When the Archbishop of Canterbury, Dr. Michael Ramsey, was in Winnipeg in 1966, I was the United Church representative who took part in the huge service of celebration (thanks to the fact that general council was being held at that time and the president of conference was away). The service was televised nationwide on CBC, and as we were lining up for a very formal procession, I found myself immediately ahead of the Knights of Columbus, with their sheathed swords. Turning to one of the K of C group, I said that if any of the Orange Lodge people from Donegal were to see me on television along with the Knights of Columbus, they would have a fit! “Well, let’s give them something to talk about then,” he said, unsheathing his sword, and putting the bare blade dangerously close to my neck! There is a sense in which we are still too close to our years at Westminster to get a proper perspective on the life and work of the congregation. Suffice it to say that in the midst of all the programs and special events that we have touched on in these last dozen pages, my own time was naturally taken up most of all with Sunday worship and pastoral care. There were many demands on one’s time: hospital visiting, as much home visiting as it was humanly possible to do, funerals, weddings, counselling, many long days and many late nights. We were blessed with very dedicated and competent staff associates, and a vast array of just as dedicated and competent lay workers. As pointed out earlier, the new board structure made it very easy to bring new people into active participation. The nominating committee was always eager to have new names passed on to them, and in my pastoral visiting. I kept an eye open for any likely candidate for a spot on the General Board. These were great years, and retirement age closed in all too quickly. But, as someone commented at our retirement party, the term should not be “re-tired,” but merely “re-treaded” for future work. It certainly turned out that way!
