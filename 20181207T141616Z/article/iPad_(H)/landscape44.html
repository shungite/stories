Part 3 Two years later, in 1904, the Assembly — which convened in St. John, NB — struck a decidedly ecumenical note, and it was there that the Union movement had its official beginning in the Presbyterian Church. Canada’s Methodist Church had shown itself similarly disposed towards union with other protestant denominations, having promoted the idea the year before at the General Conference of The Methodist Church at Winnipeg. It wasn’t however until 1925 that a merger between the two churches along with the Congregationalists, and thus the founding of the United Church, was finally achieved. Since our narrative has focused on the Presbyterian roots of our founders, a short digression to refresh our reader about the state of Congregationalism and Methodism around the time of the important 1904 Assembly: The Congregationalists originally formed as a dissident offshoot of Henry 8th’s Anglican Church. They were Puritans who no longer believed that the ostensibly corrupt Church of England could be reformed or “purified,” and 35 of them, who became known as the Pilgrim Fathers, arrived in North America on the Mayflower. But from the great cauldron of nonconformism in America, brewing without the direction of a central ecclesiastic authority, predictably there emerged over the next two centuries new schisms within the established denominations. Some of these schisms were the organic product of evolution in liturgical customs according to region, while others had a firm theological basis. The Reformation’s confidence in the integrity of the individual’s conscience, a confidence shared by the Puritans, bore at least a superficial affinity with the Enlightenment’s emphasis on the individual’s natural capacity for independent thinking and meaningfully exercised freedom. Both philosophies had certainly provided intellectual sources of much rebellion in Europe against established authority. The influence of the teachings of 16th century Dutch theologian Jacob Arminius — which, in opposition to gloomy Calvinist notions of predestination, optimistically affirmed the freedom of the human will to achieve salvation through a life of good works — helped to make the confluence of these two major strands of modern European thought, one religious and the other essentially secular, seem more plausible. John Wesley founded the Methodist Church in 1868 on explicitly Arminian principles, while the Congregationalist and Presbyterian Churches resisted the Arminian deviation in the Reform tradition.
