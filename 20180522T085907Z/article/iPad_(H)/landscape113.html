They moved into our manse at 120 Maryland Street … “over 40 large storm windows that had to be put on and taken off each year. It also had a furnace, a vast cave into which we shovelled a ton of coal a week.” Ernest seemed to forget the hardships of living on the Rock. These experiences that Ernest alludes to were pure Winnipeg in the 1930s. The windows of our homes were single glazed. While they kept out the mosquitoes the heat poured in in summer and the bone-chilling cold in winter. The storm windows were our answer to double-glazing. Heavy wood frames, not too difficult to set in place on a single story dwelling but awkward and exhausting to put up in fall and take down in spring where a ladder was necessary to reach the second story of many homes. Three story homes were even more difficult. And all those windows had to be cleaned every fall, both the installed ones and the storm ones. The coal shovelling was another labor intensive activity. Basements had a walled-in enclosure called a coal bin. The coal was delivered in sacks which hardy men carried up to the house foundation from the back lanes. The coal was then dumped through the ‘coal chute’ (a small window) into the bin. Usually by the ton. Sometimes more than a ton at a time. Every winter morning Father would go to the basement and shovel coal into the furnace. We wouldn’t get up till we felt the warm air rising through a maze of pipes and out of ‘registers’ in each room. In most homes, coal shovelling happened more than once every day. We simply didn’t understand the efficacy of proper insulation in our buildings. We could deal with flying insects but climate was a challenge. Howse’ found that his routine was far from simple. To start with; two sermons, Sunday morning and evening every week. Easter Sunday, always two morning services. (A practice started by Howse’s predecessor.) Seating capacity in the sanctuary was upwards to 1100 and at times that was inadequate for the congregation. Seats would be set up in the back area of the church and sound piped in. Peak Sunday attendance were at the Communion services, which, in the Scottish fashion, Westminster celebrated four times a year, following a solemn preparatory service on Friday nights.
