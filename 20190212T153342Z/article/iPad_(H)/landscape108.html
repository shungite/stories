He therefore offered the one year position of a probationer-in-training to Ernest, whose principal qualifications were knowing how to deal with boating under such conditions; plus two months of uncertain sermonizing in La Scie. Ernest admits “I faced a formidable task with the audacity of ignorance.” Ernest ended up serving three widely separate communities, accessible only by water, which required him to preach three sermons a week, two on Sunday morning and one late Sunday afternoon; those and a weekly prayer service as well. He was now 18 years old. He was also expected to visit each of his Burgeo parishioners once a month, conduct marriages and funerals (another sermon for each.) write letters for the illiterate and help them conduct their business. He even drafted a will for a dying old man and wrote a petition to the House of Parliament (NFLD) asking for repairs to the public wharf. He was also the doctor’s helper and assisted at surgeries, and amputations. In the outports if a person severally damaged a limb, getting to medical help was a often difficult to impossible. As result gangrene would set in requiring the amputation. Ernest notes that one time the odour of the rotten flesh was so bad he vomited on the spot. Burgeo was no bed of roses. Ernest’s stay at Burgeo lasted not one but two years. From there he was moved, still a probationer- in-training, to Clarke’s Beach for a year. This concluded the three year probationary period he needed to be accepted as a student for ministry. Thoughts of being a lawyer or a teacher were set aside. He was now 20 years old. His acceptance into training for ministry was a near thing. He had admitted to the examining committee to have been reading material written by Harry Emerson Fosdick. Some of the examiners felt this was much too modern and possibly suggested a want of appropriate character. One member of the committee was able to convince them otherwise. Fosdick would later be one of his teachers and his friend.
