Rev. Ernest Marshall Howse
 LET US PRAISE GOD For the memory and example of all the faithful departed who have worshiped in this church especially for the under named Servants of Christ sometimes called ‘THE CLAPHAM SECT’ who in the latter part of the XVIIIth and the early part of the XIXth Centuries laboured so abundantly for the increase of National Righteousness and the Conversion of the Heathen, and rested not until the curse of slavery was swept away from all parts of the British Dominions — Charles Grant, Zachary Macauly, Granville Sharp, Henry Thornton, John Thornton, Henry Venn, John Shore (Lord Teignmouth) Curate of Clapham, John Venn, James Stephen( Rector of Clapham), William Wilberforce.
Howse’s choice of this Thesis topic was no accident. It represents his deep concern for those whose lives were distressed in many different ways. In Westminster we saw other examples of this concern and the actions he was willing to take. Ernest Marshall Howse: V-Bundles for Britain And The World Council of Churches War broke out in 1939. By 1941 when the heavy bombing of Britain began it became apparent to women in Westminster Church that clothing would be an absolute necessity for people of “the beleaguered country.” However, Howse reports in his book Roses in December, in the early years of the war, relief clothing was not allowed to be shipped to England; food and war material only. Mrs. Donald McIntyre, of our congregation, made the point to Howse that a means to get the clothing over there should be found. The need was there. There comes a time when someone has to put the foot down and say wrong is wrong, enough is enough. Howse was such a person , as we can see from other legislative wrongs he worked to right. And he much admired Mrs. McIntyre as a kindred soul.
