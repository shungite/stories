In 1893, at a meeting of the Maritime Synod of the Presbyterian Church, Clarence preached two sermons that “revealed the Church in transition from the old theological method of preaching with its “plan of salvation” to the new and more human form arising out of the great evangelical movement …” Clarence was developing not only the style of his preaching, but also its distinct thematic focus. He undertook three missions in Canada before deciding, once and for all, to stay in his land of birth; answering a call to preach in Middle Stewiacke, NS. Nova Scotia’s rural population peaked in 1891, and was home around that time to numerous businesses of national and international importance. Scotland, by contrast, was plagued with overcrowding, poverty, infant mortality, and sickness; factors that help to explain the massive exodus of Scots during the 19th century to places like Nova Scotia and elsewhere in North America. In some regards, however, Nova Scotia must have struck our cultured British pastor as provincial, even backwards. Many of Scotland’s social ills were costs of the rapid, turbulent urbanization and industrialization it underwent in the 19th century, consistent with a pattern endemic to Western Europe in that period. Meanwhile, Nova Scotia, while it had similarly undergone a period of intensive industrialization from about the 1840s to the 1890s, was a society largely made up of farmers, fishers, and miners, and was fast approaching a period of relative stagnation from which, in some senses, it has never fully recovered. All the same, the sparsely populated Maritime landscape afforded distinct pleasures for this amateur naturalist. He borrowed a microscope from a professor at the local Agricultural College, and could often be spotted mucking about nearby swamps in search of “the real grounds for Evolution.” Friends of his hinted “he knew the inhabitants of the swamp better than the occupants of the pew.” He was also reputed to appear at church spattered with mud from his bicycle rides to work down the muddy trails that passed for roads at that time. This led him to comment that he was probably the first cleric in Nova Scotia to use “this new contraption” in the work of ministry. He had not completely given up his relationship to Albion however, and ultimately it was with a Scot he would share his life, after meeting and marrying Mary Tait on a single trip to Scotland in 1896.
