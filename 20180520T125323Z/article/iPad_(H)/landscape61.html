On the evening of June 30 Rev. Wilson of Augustine Presbyterian Church presided. In August there was a Minister in from Glasgow, Scotland, one from Kenora, Ontario plus Prof. Osborne of Manitoba College. In short every minister worth his collar and gown was present, at some point to be part of the party. The congregation? Officially it numbered 1155 persons. And the total cost of the land, building, organ, pews and furnishings was $218,000.00. Aside from all the pomp and parade of clergy the moment was the Congregation’s; a new beginning in an awesome new sanctuary with its wide expanse of unsupported ceiling, huge stain glass windows flooding it with light. brand new auditorium-style seating and a breath-taking array of organ pipes over the choir loft. David, Margaret and their family would have been as conscious as anyone of the potential that moment promised. During 1913 we built the new manse at the corner of Maryland And Purcell. The Christies were able to vacate 49 Furby and move in in November. ’13 was also the year David received his Doctor of Divinity granted him by The Presbyterian College of Halifax. The War In 1914 the Christies left in May for a three month holiday trip to Scotland, several European countries and the Mediterranean area. Bad timing possibly but World War One, “The Great War”, “The War To End All Wars” erupted in Europe. More than 450 men and 7 women from Westminster were to enlist for active service, among them Fleming, the Christies’ oldest son, at 22 years of age. He enlisted in The 100th Winnipeg Grenadiers. At home, Westminster’s club rooms, gym and showers in the church basement were opened to soldiers-in-training, and provided a home-away-from-home for more that 3000 servicemen in 1914 alone, and then continued to serve up to the end of the war. 1918 was the year we know would see the end of the conflict on November 11th. But in the earlier part of that year it was business as usual for our troops. Canadian battalions were in France, directly engaged with the German army which was finally in retreat. Fleming Christie was now a Lieutenant in charge of a troop of men, part of the 27th City of Winnipeg Battalion. He was proving himself
