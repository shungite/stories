At both Westminster United and later at Fifth Avenue Presbyterian Bonnell returned many times to the themes of Godless Communism and the anxieties he felt over developments in both Germany and Italy. These themes emerged during his Sunday sermons, his talks to the mid-week Westminster Young Men’s and Young Women’s clubs and also in public lectures he gave (admission 50 cents). These political concerns were also part of the background of Westminster’s favorite myth- Rev. Bonnell’s (church) services were so well attended that the overflow had to be held in the Tivoli Theater (across the street from the church.) Not so. The truth is that Bonnell’s services were so well attended the sanctuary couldn’t hold the crowds by itself. (Sanctuary seating at that time was approximately 1100.) Rather the overflow had to be accommodated in the main floor Lecture Hall and the Sunday School meeting area on the second floor where the audio of the service was “piped” in on speakers. This suggests that he was often attracting worshippers in the numbers of 1300. The Tivoli overflow that people speak of probably happened only twice where Westminster was the site for large gatherings of the general public ( admission 50 cents.). When Bonnell’s friend, Dr. Sherwood Eddy, †  “One of The greatest social prophets of our day” became available as a speaker the Winnipeg Y.M.C.A. and the Westminster Young Mens’ Club organized a mass meeting for men on November 19, 1933 in the afternoon. Eddy’s topic was ‘The Challenge of Russia.’ Admission 50 cents in the Sanctuary, 25 cents in the Tivoli Theatre across the street. A second meeting was held that evening for both men and women. The Tivoli, indeed, did pick up the overflow twice that day. (And, undoubtedly, charged the organizers of the rally rental as well.) “Not only is there a right to be happy, there is a duty to be happy. So much sadness exists in the world that we are all under obligation to contribute as much joy as lies within our powers” (J.S. Bonnell).
