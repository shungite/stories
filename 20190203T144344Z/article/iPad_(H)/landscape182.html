Women and the Westminster Church Community — text of a Presentation given by Mary Scott on the occasion of Westminster’s 125th Anniversary. Good morning! Thank you Sherri for letting me have some time this morning, as part of the Celebrations for the 125th Anniversary of Westminster Church. It’s a pleasure to be with you this morning and to be part of the service and to share some special history. When I heard Roy Halstead speak, probably about a year ago, about plans for the 125th Anniversary Year, and inviting contributions, I immediately thought of Babs Friesen, a wonderful friend and long time member of the Church, who had urged me to get more involved in church life, and certainly Westminster was a meaningful part of her very active life in the community. She worked for social justice on many fronts, belonging to many groups and organizations and received numerous awards including the Order of Canada and Y Women of Distinction. I just had to tell the stories of women from Westminster, how they made and continue to make, a difference to the Church and to the larger community. I spoke to Roy right then and there, and appreciate his support and that of the Anniversary committee. But it hasn’t been an easy task! There have been many written reports to read, and emails and suggestions from many of our members, for which I am grateful. I have been quite inspired by all of it. I’ve tried to keep this to under 20 minutes, but have a longer paper that can be available to those interested. First a brief history. It was in the fall of 1892 when eighteen members of St. Andrew’s Presbyterian Church discussed the possibility of forming a more centrally located congregation for the growing community. Permission was granted and the work of establishing a new church began: its first service was held on 1 January 1893 in Victoria Hall, later the Winnipeg Theatre, on Notre Dame Avenue, attended by more than 200 people. A few days later, the new congregation chose the name “Westminster” and construction of the permanent church at Notre Dame Avenue and Charlotte Street commenced. First services were held in the new church on 31 December 1893. Formal dedication services followed on 5 and 12 August 1894. By this time a women’s foreign mission society was active.
