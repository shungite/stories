This practice allowed Session to monitor the attendance at communion of every individual member and note those negligent in this duty. The congregation was well indoctrinated in the Scottish concept of the importance of communion. If we had a congregation of less that a thousand we would be disappointed. Slackers would be spoken to. Apart from that predictable Service regimen Howse found Westminster “large, extremely well organized — perhaps over-organized- congregation with a great deal of prestige in the city and the province.” As a result of our organization and prominence his involvement was far from routine. Beyond the normal Session and Deacon’s Court, the CGIT and the Choir we had the following activity groups, each with its own executive, meeting times and activity: The Young Men’s Club, The Young Women’s Club, The Women’s Association, The Women’s Missionary Society, The Pitblado Young Women’s Missionary Society, The Actimist Club, The Sewing Circle, The Tennis Club, The Badminton Club, The Bible Class and the Mission Band. Our Sunday School had four departments: Cradle Roll, Junior Congregation, Boys’ Department and Girls’ Department. The minister would have to be involved with or at least au courant with each of these entities. We have noted his Church Service duties on Sunday Mornings and evenings. The fore-going list would occupy extra of his time on Sunday afternoons and on mid-week afternoons and evenings where he would be invited to be present at or to give “talks” at meetings. Beyond this he would need time to write two sermons a week, attend to pastoral duties visiting the sick, the disabled and for course families in the congregation during times of bereavement, baptism, marriage etc. Don’t forget that the congregation was pushing 1500 in number and would peak, during Howse ministry at 1800. Howse would have seen his responsibilities as huge. He had ministerial assistants during his tenure who would assume direct responsibilities for some of this mass of activity. But that help would only release him for the role he would be expected to play in the general community of Winnipeg. Westminster was a prominent part of the general scene.
