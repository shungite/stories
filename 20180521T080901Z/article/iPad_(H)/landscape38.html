If he wasn’t so immersed in contemplating some theological problem to lose all track of his surroundings, perhaps he’d been gazing up at the imposing basilica of Cathédrale Saint- Boniface, which was by then in mid-construction. According to one report, his life was saved by his mittens, which had frozen to the edge of the hole and kept him from sinking. Now nearly 70 years old, and having endured poor health for many years, he decided to throw in the towel; his reputation as one of Winnipeg’s most respected and eminent pastors having long been secured. He retired in 1904 with a retirement allowance of $500 per annum for the following three years. At Charles’ leaving, there were 316 members in the congregation, and 67,000 people living in Winnipeg. Reverend Dr. Kilpatrick of Manitoba College then served as Interim Moderator until April 1905, when our second minister, Clarence McKinnon arrived. Pitblado, having once more moved to California, returned to our city in the summer of 1912. It was arranged that he would preach in our new building, but his health took another turn for the worse and the sermon he’d prepared was never delivered. He returned to California where he died about six months later. In December, his wife Sophie had his last two sermons, both undelivered, printed and sent to the Westminster congregation “…  for the many dear friends to whom both he and I were so deeply attached.”
