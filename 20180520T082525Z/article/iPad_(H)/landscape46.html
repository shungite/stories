Overworked, and with memories of the promising, quickly industrializing city of Winnipeg (which may have reminded him of Chicago if not Glasgow) still fresh in his mind, he was pleased to receive a call from Westminster Church in Winnipeg, which had been signed by 232 members and 210 adherents. Church notables Sidney Smith and John Bellingham appeared to have spearheaded the case to recruit MacKinnon, stressing in congregational meetings that their new minister should be “sound in doctrine, evangelical in spirit, and earnest in purpose.” Surely aware of MacKinnon’s missionary experience, impassioned style of preaching, and rigorous theological training — but likely ignorant of the copies of Marx and Darwin under his pillow — they felt he exemplified these virtues. The MacKinnons arrived on Friday, May 15 and Clarence was introduced to the congregation on Sunday, May 28th. He preached his first Westminster sermon that evening, which was based on Psalm 144 (“Happy is that people whose God is the Lord”). It appears that Smith and Bellington’s favourite candidate did not fail to inspire them. Towards the end of his tenure at Westminster, which only lasted four years, Clarence expressed his concern to church elders that many of the children in Winnipeg’s burgeoning west end were growing up without religious instruction. It was too late for Clarence to concertedly address this problem, as he was advancing into a plum position with Pine Hill Theological Seminary, so he enjoined the congregants gathered to do something about it. Smith and Bellingham rose to the challenge, moving their bible class from Westminster to a small building at Ellice Avenue and Beverly Street.
