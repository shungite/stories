Minnie arrived to Winnipeg at a time when accommodation was scarce and poverty already severe, and her considerable contributions to Winnipeg’s welfare — through the blankets, food, medical attention, and lodgings, that she worked over a half century to provide to single mothers, soldiers, workers, and the poor — speak for themselves.
The Imperial Order Daughters of the Empire (IODE), one of the most prominent Canadian charities of the first half of the 20th century, emerged out of the wave of patriotism that swept across the British Empire following its provocation of the devastating Second Boer War. Canadian Margaret Polson Murray was in England during the war’s outbreak, and was greatly affected by the English public’s outpouring of support for Britain’s colonial war. Upon her return to Canada, she set immediately to work on founding a woman’s network that would “place themselves in the front rank of colonial patriotism and give practical charitable aid to soldiers, and if they were killed, support for their dependants, and care for their graves.” Following Murray’s lead, a few women members of London’s high society established a sister organization, the Victoria League; fully intending, however, to subordinate the IODE as though it were its younger sibling: as members of the colony, the ladies of the IODE were seen in a socially inferior light. A rivalry between the two groups ensued; the same imperialist mindset that animated the groups’ initial showing of colonial solidarity now working ironically to undermine that sentiment. In any case, the rivalry does not appear to have diminished the growth of the IODE or its loyalties. By the end of the Second World War, it counted 50,000 members, including Minnie Campbell who was by then one of its most influential members.
