In the meantime, the land was filling up with newcomer Europeans and Asians. Houses and commercial buildings were needed — and, to accommodate the religious faiths these people brought with them, so were places of worship. The Scots, Presbyterians all, had been around Assiniboia since the beginning of the century. Only latterly did a minister of their faith arrive to the Red River Colony. His name was George Bryce. In 1871, he was selected by the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in Canada to come west to the Colony to organize a Presbyterian college and a new church. He set up Manitoba College in the same year and taught there until 1909. A year after his selection, he founded Knox Church, the first Presbyterian church in Winnipeg. Our story of Westminster’s community begins there. The first Knox Presbyterian Church, built in 1868, was located at the corner of Fort Street and Portage Avenue, the second at the intersection of Princess Street and Ellice Avenue. As the congregations’ numbers increased sharply over the next decade in step with city growth, Winnipeg’s Presbyterians faced a problem that many congregations would today welcome: the need to expand. They resolved to build a third house of worship. St. Andrew’s Church had its start in 1881 on the corner of Elgin Avenue. and Ellen St., but by the end of that decade, it faced the same problem of overcrowding. So, in 1892, a group within its congregation decided that a new Presbyterian church was needed closer to the city centre. That was us. The story is simple enough. Eighteen members of that group met together in the law offices of Colin Campbell, a prominent citizen and member of our congregation. After some discussion they drafted a petition to the Winnipeg Presbytery to establish a new church. The following people signed the document and it was sent off to Presbytery.
