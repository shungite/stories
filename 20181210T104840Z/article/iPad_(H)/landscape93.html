Fosdick saw the progress/development of the human race not as an aimless meandering, a wanton dallying in whirlpools of activity leading nowhere. Rather he saw what others have called the Kingdom of God as the ultimate goal for the development of human wholeness. Fosdick was, in no sense, an originator of these ideals. But he was an influential exponent of Liberal Christianity. Bonnell was solidly of a mind with Liberal Christianity. In an issue of Look  magazine (a popular American newsmagazine of the 1930s through the 1950s) He wrote a major article on the Presbyterian Church of his day is which he echoed Fosdick’s rejection of of traditional Christian doctrines including Christ’s virgin birth, the inerrancy of Scripture, and the literal return of Jesus Christ. Bonnell, too, looked for a Kingdom of God as did Fosdick. Fundamentalist William Jennings Bryan characterized Fosdick’s preaching as “utter agnosticism”. The fundamentalist church renounced Bonnell as The Devil’s minister. Bonnell connected with Fosdick in other respects. Bonnell wrote the book Psychology for Pastor and People — A Book on Spiritual Counselling (1948). Fosdick wrote the book On Being a Real Person  (1943). Both books were influenced by the writing of Freud and Jung and both encouraged the pastor as a counsellor. Psychology ,as a form of medical intervention, was of growing interest for its healing properties. But what of the healing properties of religious faith? Both Fosdick and Bonnell felt the need to stress this link with the secular medical practice. Bonnell went one step further in his book in that he cautioned ministers against confusing religion with medicine. Ministers, in their enthusiasm for the god-like role they thought doctor’s were playing, attempted to treat their congregants for schizophrenia, depression etc. Stop this, Bonnell said. We are not trained for medical practice. Our practice is God’s love, God’s healing grace. The interesting difference between the two men is that Fosdick, as a practicing minister, did no pastoral visiting, and limited his counselling contact time with a congregant to 15 minutes per visit. For Bonnell on the other hand, counselling was an integral part of his ministry and his individual sessions were as long those of psychiatrist or psychological counsellors in the secular area.
