Ernest Marshall Howse on Antisemitism In the 1930s antisemitism was a serious, developing issue in Europe as it had become part of the Nazi Party’s avowed policy in Germany. The harassment and persecution of the Jews was being reported in North America with chilling frequency and Americans and Canadians were either not listening or were inclined to discount the reports as exaggeration. In short, antisemitism was alive and/or unnoticed (quietly ignored?) in Canada; a disquieting thought. From time to time it was “deplored” from the pulpit by some of the more eminent of Canadian preachers. As mentioned elsewhere Howse was a prominent advocate for the social gospel movement and, as many of his actions illustrated, a social activist. In 1939 he preached two sermons which resonated far from the walls of our Church to the national Church and beyond. The following lengthy quotation from the book How Silent Were The Churches? Canadian Protestantism And The Jewish Plight During The Nazi Era  (Davis and Nefsky), covers his theme in those sermons: In April 1939 Howse preached, in Westminster Church, two sermons on the refugee situation. They were based on the Matthew text (25:40) “Inasmuch as you have done it onto the least of my brethren, you have done it onto me.” The sermons were broadcast on the radio, and later published in Winnipeg. Both referred explicitly though not exclusively to the Jews, and both called for the relaxation of the rigid immigration laws hindering their sanctuary in Canada. The refugees, Howse declared, particularly the Jewish refugees, far from swamping the country with undesirable types, would prove to be “refined gold from a fierce crucible.” He further referred to them as belonging to “the most highly trained and gifted families in Europe. Their only offence is to belong to the race that gave us Isiah, Jesus, and St. Paul.” For this effort he received many letters which he characterized as bitterly hostile. Canadian immigration policy remained very restrictive toward the victims of Hitler’s Germany, but Howse was not a voice alone. The month before Howse spoke up, the Rev. Claris Silcox, secretary of the Christian Social Service Council spoke on Canada’s refugee policy to a group at University of Toronto.
