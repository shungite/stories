We had 80 fewer Home Mission Fields.           “When our members become aware of this situation there will be a magnificent response           that will wipeout deficits and send us forward once more to play a worthy part           in the consumation of the Kingdom Of God in this and other lands. 5 1931 / The Congregation used Mission Band MITE BOXES. These were small cardboard boxes       to collect small change to give to the poor. People were encouraged to bring them to church every       Sunday to deposit that week’s collection. (Remember that this was in the Great Depression.       For some people ‘small change’ was a big deal.) 6 1933 / Cent-A-Meal boxes had replaced Mite Boxes and were being used by 240 homes in       our congregation. During this year and later we operated a Good Neighbours Club, a day shelter       for unemployed single men. ‘Unemployed single men’ were to be found right across the country,       but especially in the West where drought was closing down farms and limiting employment       opportunities. Victims of both the Depression and drought, these people would hop on freight       trains and travel from city to city. Anywhere they heard there might be jobs. Staying alive       was a desperate business. 7 In 1951 (and many years after) every Sunday a group of AOTS men under the leadership of Keith Waddington (pictured) went to the Municipal Hospitals where they assisted wheel- chair patients attend their own church service in the hospital chapel. Sunday mornings, sitting in the choir loft, we could see the men come rushing in to our service after their work was done. 8 In June of 1966 our Sewing Circle shipped six large cartons of clothing were to Korea.       The Group had not only collected but also prepared the goods for shipment. 9 In the 1930s we supplied materials for rural Sunday Schools, Christmas hampers for the needy,       Hallowe’en costumes for UNICEF and supported a boy in Java through Foster Parents Plan.
