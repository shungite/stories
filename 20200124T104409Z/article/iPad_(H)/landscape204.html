During these years in full ministry, the respect Nelson garnered in his profession was attested to by his being asked to serve as President of Manitoba and Northwestern Ontario Conference in 1960. Then, in 1974, he was asked to serve as President  of the Alberta and Northwest Conference. Further to this unusual recognition for a minister to be asked to be President of two Conferences, Nelson was presented in 1962 with the honorary degree of Doctor of Divinity by his Alma Mater, St. Andrew’s College. And finally he was a member of the Senate of St. Stephan’s College in Edmonton (a theological school that conducts post baccalaureate professional and academic degree programs to  men and women for the practice of ministry). After his retirement (and later his death in January of 1991), two public documents cited particular strengths in Nelson’s work aside from his standard ministerial activities. In an in memoriam document the following: Nelson was a widely read scholar and a recognized pastoral theologian …  Further on in the document the following: … his deep concern and care for persons, etc. These statements resonate with us in relation to Nelson’s work with Westminster. Grace Hospital was located a few blocks from the Church in the Wolseley area. Nelson was a well known visitor of patients there, not only to people who were members of Westminster congregation but to some whose connection was tenuous. The writer’s mother, for example was in Grace dying of cancer. She had no connection to Westminster other than that of her son being a Choir member of the Church. Yet one afternoon a “tall, grey haired, distinguished looking gentleman” visited her, an event that drew the staff’s particular attention and mother’s complete delight. And later my admiration for Nelson’s initiative. Our Assistant Minister at the time, Donald Keating, was very insistent that the Church should move out of the building and into the community. (Donald initiated House Services of Worship in homes in the local community.) Nelson, in his own way, and playing to his personal strengths as a warm human being, spent a great deal of his time “moving out of the Church,” simply visiting the ill and the elderly, wherever they were.
