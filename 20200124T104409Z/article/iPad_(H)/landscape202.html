The Story of Nelson Mercer Preface Alan Huband, our first Post War II Minister from the late 1940s to 1955 faced challenges to which his predecessors — Rev. Christie, Rev. Bonnell and Rev. Howse — would scarcely have given thought. The heady years of our congregation’s expansion (about 1910 to 1939) were  behind us. Yes, under Dr. Howse, we had barely maintained our numbers and our vigour as a leading Winnipeg church through to the end of the War. However, post-war Canada evidenced changes in our society (and the Church) which were to impact on our own congregation. Dr. Huband had inherited a Church which had lost some of its energy and its sense of its leadership in the Winnipeg Community. People were questioning their ideals and the verities they once had accepted as norms. Technology was beginning to change communication (Winnipeg’s first television broadcast was in 1952). We wanted to know why we shouldn’t watch major league game events on Sunday; why we weren’t able to shop on Sunday; why alcoholic beverages were not more widely available, and so on. All these questions and more were being asked, not just in the wider population, but in our congregation and on Sunday mornings, too. Westminster looked, hopefully, at petitioning the Provincial Government to maintain yesterday’s norms. Meanwhile we had to stand by and watch as the world, and our own lives, changed. And as our congregation started its decline in size and influence. In the spring of 1955 Rev. Huband gave notice that he had been called to serve in a large urban Toronto church as of September. This necessitated our forming a search committee to find his successor. In June the Committee called a congregational meeting to announce that they had found the successor. The meeting was held following the morning service. Huband, as was the custom, was not present in the sanctuary. However, there was a speaker in the Men’s Choir Room back of the sanctuary (unknown to the Committee) and the Minister stationed himself there to hear the Committee's announcement.
