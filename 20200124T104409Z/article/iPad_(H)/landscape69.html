We lay in suspense for about an hour anxiously waiting for the Australians to move on. The tanks and cavalry at last did the trick, and the hillside which had proved such a tough nut to crack was at last covered with flying Huns. We could move on again in safety and did so, until for the first time our air superiority was challenged by a huge patrol of Hun plances. For a while they bombed us and machine gunned us, and made us wish we were anywhere but the position we were in. Our airmen soon gathered in force, and after a tremendous battle drove the Hun away. It was a glorious sight, but what a tragic one, to see plane after plane crash to the ground in flames. We were now way ahead of any active artillery support, but the tanks furnished our barrage, or rather took the place of it, and until nightfall we still kept advancing, though at a much slower rate in face of the increasing opposition. Two of our companies were now in the first wave and did good work, but when the halt was called for the day our turn had not come, so throughout we did not come to close quarters with the Hun. Most of us knew the advance was nearing its end. Strong counter attacks were being thrown in, and the effect of the surprise was now over, as the Hun reserves were rapidly coming up. The Writing on the Wall As I write, both sides have settled down for the time being. What the near future holds for us we do not of course know. But the Hun has already been badly defeated, has lost immense stores of materials and guns, and suffered many casualties; and this push which has more than placed Amiens out of shell fire is certainly a bright omen for us, and the handwriting on the wall for Fritz, when next spring we have three million Americans to press our attack right through. It seems to me that we would have been able to get right through to the River Somme with very little trouble, had we only had the necessary number of fresh troops to keep him on the run.
