We (Howse) took stands on daily issues, local, provincial, national and international. He was expected to give addresses to clubs and lodges, and hold membership in boards and societies.   And he did have a family. Esther and Margaret were soon joined by David and George, both born in Winnipeg. He mentions in his book, Roses in December , a two month holiday with pay in the summer… .For me that was a life preserver … a time to do extra reading and writing. My years were marked not from January to January but from vacation to vacation. At the end of January I was “half way to the lake.” (A phrase he found peculiar to Winnipegers apparently — elsewhere in Canada people go to the cottage — we normally go to the lake. Or so says Ernest.) I am closing off this essay at this point. The number of political and social issues in which Ernest Marshall Howse was involved was considerable and deserves separate articles to cover some of the details. Some of these activities impinged directly on Westminster. Other involved only Howse as a lone public figure. As such he was both praised and damned. That comes of taking stands on issues. Howse loved issues. He was never quiet where he felt his voice needed to be heard. Article by R. Halstead- with thanks for those items more or less directly borrowed from Roses in December. Our 50th Anniversary Recording Voices from 1943 The original recording — 78 rpm on ‘wax’ — has  long since been lost. However someone with a sense of History thought to re-record it on cassette tapes, which we in turn have digitized. The cassettes come to us courtesy of Hedley Auld, son of Isabel and Murray Auld. The occasion of the recording was the 1943 50th Anniversary of the Congregation. The point of the recording was that it contained messages from the past which would be played to the congregation on its 100th Anniversary in 1992. By 1992 the fact of the recording had been forgotten and it has never been heard by the congregation since its creation. Until today, by you.
