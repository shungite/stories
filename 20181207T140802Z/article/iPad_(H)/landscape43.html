With uncharacteristic venom Clarence comments on Riel: “infatuated half-breed  … in a moment of madness shot Thomas Scott … irresponsible person … half-demented troubler of the West …” (Thomas Scott was a Canadian-Irish Orangeman and adherent of the ultra-Protestant Canadian Party, which promoted the annexation of the Red River Colony by the Canadian government and settlement of Anglophones in Manitoba. Riel executed him after he and other affiliates of the Canadian Party attacked Upper Fort Garry, then occupied by Riel’s provisional government.) Three years later, in the same month he was inducted to Park Street Church in Halifax, The Boer War in South Africa broke out, and Clarence noted with approval the large number of young Canadians who were crossing the Atlantic to fight with the British. Clarence may not have been English by either descent or birth, but exhibited the “respectable” degree of loyalism for someone in his station. In 1902, Clarence travelled by train to Vancouver to attend the General Assembly of the Canadian Presbyterian Church. While it was from these assembles that the United Church was eventually born, the most notable detail of this trip from the point of our narrative was Clarence’s brief stop in Winnipeg, which left a very favourable impression upon him: “Presently the train rumbled over a low bridge across the sluggish and muddy Red River …  to the first primitive CPR station of Winnipeg … the amazing city that as by the magic of an Aladdin’s lamp was springing into being in this new country … all was a-hum with human activity. Enterprise and energy were evident on every side. The hotels were crowded … We were fortunate to secure a room in the top story of the Leland Hotel … one can never forget the view from its windows of the vast expanse of green and treeless prairie stretching from the city limits to the far horizon. We were on the edge of that wonderland where the past counted for little and the future was all in all … ” The bug of Western Expansionism had nipped MacKinnon.
