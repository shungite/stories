My intention in preparing the material here is to shed light on subjects that, to varying degrees, escaped the notice of previous preparers of anniversary books. I have tried to focus, for example, on: 1)   A diverse range of vital Westminster figures, both our official leaders those who did and do so much to keep Westminster operating and animated on a day-to-day basis. In this narrative clerical workers emerge alongside clerics, labourers in the vineyard alongside speakers of the pulpit, as central figures. 2)   Why a gymnasium was included in the design of our building. As I attempt to illustrate, this feature reflects an early 20th century national obsession with youth athletics, and that this obsession was motivated by widespread confidence that our country’s bright future would be secured by young white, Anglo Saxon and Protestant men, whose spiritual and physical superiority would symbolize their natural leadership qualities. So, the thinking went, and as a community we’d better ensure that Roman Catholic boys didn’t outshine our young Protestant athletes! 3)   How our Sunday School developed and why a host of mid-week activities got tacked on. 4)   Our music programs. In previous books, the topic of music at Westminster was sidelined with such phrases as “… and the choir presented a lovely program for us.” As a chorister of over 65 years in this church, I think our work is worth more than that condescending afterthought. 5)   What our ministers have said to us in the past — not just their words but how they might have sounded pounding out their homilies from the pulpit! 6)   The place of women in the congregation’s life; not only the paid women leaders, but also the women volunteers who’ve been so indispensable throughout Westminster’s history to our direction and wellbeing
