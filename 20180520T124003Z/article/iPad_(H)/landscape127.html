The other was the very loud and very dogmatic Rev. Claris Silcox who, after WWII, became an avowed anti-Zionist. Silcox considered the Balfour Declaration (which promised the Jews a national homeland in Palestine) a mistake and described the U.N. Partition plan of 1947 as “utterly stupid”….he said it would bring about “a more bitter antisemitism than this world has ever known.” And Ernest Marshall Howse agreed with him. How can one reconcile these seemingly contradictory positions: love of Jews, and help for Jewish refugees, and, on the other hand, strong opposition to the Zionist idea and to the state of Israel? That was the question supporters of Silcox, and certainly of Howse were asking. And, if anti-Zionism is equated with anti-semitism and it was, most definitely, then … Howse in his book, Roses In December, doesn’t deal with any of this controversial position in which he found himself. An open question, still. The writer of this piece doesn’t see Howse as antisemitic. Howse’ actions during WWII and the manner in which he deported himself in other situations dealing with people who were needing support shows a man of incredible intellect, a genuine love for the Human Family, and a vision of world peace, which, says one commentator, he shared with the ancient Jewish prophets. (R.H.)
