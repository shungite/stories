(The text of the constitution is in my Westminster file.) I have included this summary of our church board restructuring because it changed in a major way the focus of my work at Westminster. It gave a whole new direction to the life and work of the congregation. I was really impressed with the way in which a structure, or system of government, can change the life of a church. For many years, the congregation seemed to be pulled in three directions by the Session, by the UCW, and by the Stewards. Now, there was only one board. There were no financial secrets tucked away in the Stewards’ safekeeping. There was no Session, so any members of the congregation could be, and were, asked to serve the elements on Communion Sunday. The UCW had no secret agenda, because there was no UCW! The units were still meeting, but they came under the fellowship committee. There were still “districts” and “district visiting,” but the visitation was arranged by the membership and visiting committee. And, best of all, we were finding new people every year to become board members, and all the board members were aware of all the work in the church. In short, we became a much more open society. And with this new breath of fresh air blowing in the church, it gave me new impetus, and was the chief reason, I think, why I found it easy to continue on at Westminster for a few more years, and eventually till retirement when I reached age sixty-five in 1979. There were changes in our household, too. Doug, who had enrolled in Grade 9 at River Heights Junior High when we came to Winnipeg in 1963, went on to Kelvin High and then to do his BA at the University of Winnipeg, graduating with the gold medal for highest standing in the general arts course. He had worked each summer (on the end of a hoe) at the University of Manitoba farm, and had a bank account to go to York University in Toronto in the fall of 1970 to pursue a PhD in psychology. So, in our huge manse at 141 Waterloo Street (3,200 square feet of floor space and a 100 × 150 foot property), we were down to one family member at home. Rob had proceeded through elementary, junior high, and was now (1970–1971) in his final year at Kelvin High School and had made his decision to go east for his university work.
