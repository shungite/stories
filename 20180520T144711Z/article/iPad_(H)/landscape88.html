At Westminster, all these themes ; the decline of the social order , the efficacy of Religious faith, the threat of Communism, Fascism, Socialism, Nazism, Bonnell not only covered these on Sunday mornings and evenings, but also, on a regular basis, on Tuesday evenings when our Young Men’s Club met. His topics for these weekly gatherings were often announced in the preceding Sunday’s bulletin enticing people to hear his message. Beyond this activity we see references to public lectures he gave in 1934 (admission, 50 cents). On Wednesdays at 5:45 PM, he had a weekly radio program on station CKY. At one point, while with us, he headed west to Saskatchewan to conduct a week-long evangelical crusade in Regina. He was a tireless advocate for God’s peace, a remedy for human tribulation. And people listened, in great numbers. Yes there was no television. Yes radio was still in its infancy. Yes movies were just beginning to add sound to the pictures (Walt Disney’s Micky Mouse in Steamboat Willie , 1928, the first.) But the World was an uncertain place; unrest in Europe, unemployment in Canada. Bonnell was a commanding presence, his speaking authoritative, his message, hope. Bonnell’s U.S. Ministry: New Opportunities, New Successes Westminster was Bonnell’s third assignment (call). In 1928, at the end of his second assignment, St. Andrew’s Presbyterian in St. John, New Brunswick he was given a public commendation by that city for his preaching and his contribution in general to the city. In April of 1935, at the end of his service at Westminster, the Canadian Radio Commission “in recognizing the signal honour that had come to Dr. Bonnell in being invited to an international pulpit” gave him the opportunity to make a coast to coast broadcast. This, in Canada, was a singular honour to be given a minister of the Christian faith. But, over all, Bonnell’s work in Canadian churches seems to have been a prelude to the greater challenge and greater public recognition accorded him in his appointment to Fifth Avenue Presbyterian Church in New York City. Fifth Avenue Presbyterian was, by all accounts a prestigious church and pulpit.
