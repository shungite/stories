Turning to one of the K of C group, I said that if any of the Orange Lodge people from Donegal were to see me on television along with the Knights of Columbus, they would have a fit! “Well, let’s give them something to talk about then,” he said, unsheathing his sword, and putting the bare blade dangerously close to my neck! There is a sense in which we are still too close to our years at Westminster to get a proper perspective on the life and work of the congregation. Suffice it to say that in the midst of all the programs and special events that we have touched on in these last dozen pages, my own time was naturally taken up most of all with Sunday worship and pastoral care. There were many demands on one’s time: hospital visiting, as much home visiting as it was humanly possible to do, funerals, weddings, counselling, many long days and many late nights. We were blessed with very dedicated and competent staff associates, and a vast array of just as dedicated and competent lay workers. As pointed out earlier, the new board structure made it very easy to bring new people into active participation. The nominating committee was always eager to have new names passed on to them, and in my pastoral visiting. I kept an eye open for any likely candidate for a spot on the General Board. These were great years, and retirement age closed in all too quickly. But, as someone commented at our retirement party, the term should not be “re-tired,” but merely “re-treaded” for future work. It certainly turned out that way! Perhaps a brief review of our own family should be given here, for during this sixteen-year tenure at Westminster, many things had happened. Miss Marion Forbes had been with us since 1950, moving along as part of our family and household from Edmonton to Regina to Winnipeg. But, the time came when she didn’t want to be alone in the house when we were away, and in 1968, with Fran’s wedding in London in the offing, she moved into the very fine (and new) Fred Douglas Lodge. We were always in close touch with her, and she was back to the house for special events, as her health permitted. Following an attack of pneumonia in the summer of 1970, she had to be moved to a nursing home for a month or two. I was with her when she died peacefully on October 2, 1970. She had been an important part of our whole family, and we were always grateful that our home could be her home for twenty years.
