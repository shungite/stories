Rev. Ernest Marshall Howse
On Memories: “That is the nature of human existence. Life in Blackhead remains reality while I and others live to recall it; but when our memories are no more, part of what we saw and much of what we were will be gone forever. Every human being takes to eternity some small part of time.”
From Blackhead the family moved to Bonavista in 1913. Ernest was 10 years old. The town held a population of over 4000 people. Charles Howse was not the only minister in town. His Methodist ministry share was nearly half the population, the Church of England equally present and the rest was a smattering of Roman Catholics as well. The Howses moved into a new parsonage which looked out on a small lake (just a broadening of the river) that in NFLD was called a pond. They had the usual chickens and a cow to help provide sustenance. The cow, Gwen, was Earnest’s responsibility. He speaks of getting up at 6:00 AM, milking her, cleaning out the stable and setting her off into the woods for free pasture. In the afternoon, when he got home from school he would have to set off into the woods himself to find her and drive her home. While they were in Bonavista WW I broke out. NFLD is proud of the number of its young men who served in that War. In those times going to War was an adventure, a duty, an honour, a privilege to fight for King and Country. Whereas, Ernest observes, later on in WW 2 there was no adventure and glory. “They knew that it was a repulsive business. But, facing Hitler and fascism, they felt they had no alternative.”
