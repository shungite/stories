Dr. Ernest Marshall Howse , our minister from 1935 to 1946. Dr. Howse was the ‘incumbent’       minister at the time of the Anniversary. In his message he pays particular note to WWII and       the conditions it was creating in Canada as well as elsewhere. By the sound of his voice we       are reminded of his early background as a Newfoundlander. During his message Mrs. Howse       is invited to speak. Articles on Howse are to be found elsewhere in this publication. 5.   Isaak Pitblado . He, too, represents 1893 as his father was our first minister and Isaak was       a young man at that time (see picture in the Pitblado article). His message gives us the rare       sound of his father’s first sermon to the congregation and the accent the congregation       might have heard from Charles Bruce Pitblado himself. Ernest Marshall Howse vs Government During his time with us, particularly during the period (1939-45) of WW II, and earlier, Howse became known as a small L ‘liberal,’ a voice against bad government, and against causes which were unfair or damaging to various groups of people. His reputation in this regard was recognized, not only in Winnipeg, but more generally across the whole of Canada. In another article in this series we mention one of his major issues with Government: “One cause to which I was ardently committed was the struggle to shift the Canadian Government to a more humane policy on refugees — particularly the Jewish refugees fleeing from the monstrous cruelty of the Nazis.” The article is called Antisemitism . The following piece describes Howse at war (1) with the Quebec Government and (2) with the Alberta Government and (3) with the Federal Government concerning other bad acts of legislation.
