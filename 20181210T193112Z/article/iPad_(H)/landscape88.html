In the centre of the little group making its way into Corinth was a man of medium stature, with no physical beauty to commend him, but who by his every word and gesture revealed a dynamic personality …” A massive introduction to a small man, Saul, who had become Paul. During Bonne||’s service at Westminster Church — the period of the Great Depression — his sermons frequently reflected his concern for social order. But while he preached on topics such as ‘The Human Factor in Unemployment,’ and ‘Youth and the Liquor Question,’ there was an underlying concern in all his preaching for the remedy to these issues, Christian faith. The title of another sermon says it all: ‘The Suicide of the Social Order; The Reign of God is the Only Hope of The World.’ To quote Bonnell on this imperative: “When Rome’s youth became debased and enervated (drained of vitality), when regard was lost for men’s honour and women’s purity, when the sanctity of the home was violated, when her literature became cynical and debased, her dominion ended. The moral life of any people rises or falls with the vitality or decay of its religious life.’’ While I may question how much Bonnell did or didn’t know about Rome’s religious life under the caesars I can hear him writing that paragraph as a metaphor of the twentieth century. He was painfully aware of the Communist threat to Christianity and the devastating effects of the Depression on social order. He was also aware of the influence of writings of prominent authors such as the English, Bertrand Russell ,and the American, Ernest Hemingway. These men and others like them seemed to him to advocate throwing all morals overboard. Was his anxiety well founded? Within the span of his lifetime he would have seen the beginnings of a decline in people’s interest in worship and religious commitment as he understood it. By the 1950s we at Westminster were already debating the concept of Sunday as“a day of rest”, timidly looking for ice—cream parlours to be open on the sabbath, considering the question of professional sporting events as Sunday diversion. As we said good—bye to Rev. AllanHuband and looked forward to the coming of Rev. Nelson Mercer (1955) we looked hopefully to see a turn-around to the decline in our membership which followed the Second World War. It was not to be.
