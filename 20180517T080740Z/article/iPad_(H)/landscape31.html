Stories of Westminster United Church & its People / Page 31
But back to our dear Reverend: Charles and his wife Sofie, along with their four sons (left to right in the picture: Charles, John, Isaac, and Will) arrived in Winnipeg June 7, 1882. Their daughter Lillie, also shown in the picture, was born two years later, making her about four years old in this photo from the Pitblado family archives. A year after he arrived the Reverend, together with his friend and associate Principal John M. King (head of the Manitoba College) and eminent merchant William Ashdown Sr., joined the Historical and Scientific Society of Manitoba. In 1885, Charles took another one of his leaves of absence to serve as chaplain to the 90th Rifles, providing religious counsel to the regiment brought from Eastern Canada to fight the Métis insurgents in the North-West Rebellion. To spot Pitblado in the picture note the three men in front just right of centre. He is standing directly behind them with clerical collar. This picture (on the following page) was taken at Fish Creek, now in Saskatchewan, where the battle started on May 19. Three days later, at the Battle of Batoche, the Métis ran out of ammunition and the combat ended shortly thereafter; with Louis Riel arrested and sent on his way to Regina for trial. The Pitblado family archives contain information that Pitblado accompanied Riel on the journey and that he spent evenings conversing with the devoutly Catholic insurgent. Family records also suggest that Pitblado gave evidence at the trial. However, the trial records include a list of all the witnesses, both prosecution and defense, and Pitblado’s name is not among them. No doubt he was there, and it’s probable that he talked outside the courtroom to many of the people that were recorded witnesses. (The picture shows Riel in the witness box.) The Métis and an associated group of First Nation Cree and Assiniboine had waged the battle. Riel and eight First Nation leaders were hung.
