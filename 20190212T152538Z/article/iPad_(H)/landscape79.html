During his career as a minister/pastor Bonnell developed a blend of psychological and spiritual guidance about which he wrote several books encouraging other ministers to employ the knowledge and techniques of psychology in their pastoral work. Another theme that ran through his sermons and talks had to do with Protestantism, the meaning of the word, the need for a union of protestant denominations, and the root of his differences with Rome. On the meaning of the word Protestant Bonnell notes, “Most people think that it means merely an Objector, one who is opposing beliefs or policies. That idea is, of course, quite wrong. A protestant, as the use of the word in Reformation times reveals, means one who makes a protestation … a solemn declaration of fact opinion or resolution.” He goes on to say (in the Old testament),  “… the prophets of Israel were the Protestants of the Hebrew faith.” Bonnell was concerned about divisions in various Protestant denominations. He worried that we were under attack from Communism and Fascism and that we needed to stand firm together. His stand against union with Rome was another matter entirely. Concerning this … On Christmas day, 1931, Pope Pius XI issued his encyclical Lux Veritatus, The Light of Truth . (On the question of reunion between Roman, Eastern Orthodox and Protestant faiths), the Pope invited them to make their submission to him on two grounds: first the Immaculate Conception of The Virgin Mary and secondly the dogma of Papal Infallibility. For Bonnell, submission to such ideas was anathema. Bonnell believed, as did St. Paul, in Jesus as the Chief Cornerstone of the Church. No room for Rome’s immaculate conceptions and such business in John Sutherland Bonnell’s church. During his time at Westminster Bonnell preached on the
Oxford Movement in the Anglican Church
. It’s ironic that the Oxford movement was what might be called the Catholic revival in the Church of England which argued against the increasing secularization of the Church of England, and sought to recall it to its heritage of apostolic order, and to the catholic doctrines of the early church fathers. One wonder’s how Bonnell squared his antipathy for Rome’s theology with his interest in the heritage of the apostolic order.
