If our story is to continue a question has to be asked: “Which Rev. Charles Bruce Pitblado”? The reason for this is that there were two men called Charles Bruce Pitblado. They both lived in Salmon River, Nova Scotia. They both became ministers, one of the Methodist Church and one of the Presbyterian Church. They were first cousins, one the son of James, the other the son of John. The Methodist took his training in the New England states and ended up in Boston (which suggests that James might have spent time south of the border after the Oak Island incident). This Charles Bruce, the Methodist, married a Scottish lass, Effie Wilson, from Edinburgh, who became a prominent suffragette and public speaker throughout the U.S.. They had two sons. The other Charles Bruce is, of course, the burden of our story, which now continues! In 1865, he accepted the position of Pastor at St. Mary’s, at Glenelg in the mining district of Guysboro County, Nova Scotia, where he served for six years. He was married by this time to Sophie Christie, daughter of Isaac Christie and Susanna Quill (Isaac was a farmer near Salmon River). The Pitblados had five children: John (1865-1946), Isaac (1867-1964), Charles Bruce (1870-1871), Charles Bruce (1873-1913), George (1877-1938), Lillian (1884-1972) The two Charles Bruces in this list are no accident. The first died as an infant. The second was elected to carry on the name. In 1871, Charles Bruce then became the pastor of Chalmers Church in Halifax. Eight years after his beginning, an article appeared in the local paper: “Eight years ago a thunder bolt struck the spire of Chalmers Church and wrecked the building sadly. A few months before that time all the wealthier and more fashionable members and adherents became connected with Fort Massey Church — leaving the old congregation a poor but hardworking fragment. It was in response to the call of this poor little congregation, occupying this thunder-stricken Church, that Rev. C.B. Pitblado came to this city.” The article goes on to describe Charles as “a Scotchman … brought up on oatmeal porridge, the Shorter Catechism, hard work, and the Psalms of David in metre.” Of his preaching, the article’s author, bearing the nom de plume Homespun, says “… he has not preached a dull or heartless sermon since he came to Halifax … his forte is in clear statement and in rich, fresh, racy illustration … his voice is often harsh, strained and pitched in a false key (ie-high pitch). It would be an immense advantage to himself and his hearers if he would learn to modulate his voice properly.” We are told that Chalmers was not a “fashionable” church. The people dressed plainly and there were no instruments to accompany the singing.
