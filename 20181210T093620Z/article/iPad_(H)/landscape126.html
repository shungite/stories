But in the end, he said, the cause was “the existence throughout Canada of a latent antisemitism….” There was no question about it, however. The core issue was the recalcitrance of the MacKenzie King government to relax its policy; call it antisemitic or what you will. Howse relates how the pressure on the Government was relentless.  Senator, Carine Wilson organized a Canadian National Committee on Refugees. She then managed to organize branches in major Canadian cities. In Winnipeg she contacted Howse who helped organize a group here (and served as its Chair.) They managed eventually, “through the tedious slow process of wartime bureaucracy “ to bring one group of six families over, one Christian and the other five Jewish. Shortly thereafter the Government turned all refugee considerations over to the UN. So that first group was the last. § Howse efforts on behalf of Jews was not restricted to refugee concerns. Certain associations he had had in Beverly Hills had opened his conscience to bridging “old divisions and risking new adventures in inter-faith harmony.” To start with, in 1940, through the auspices of B’nai Brith Howse joined Roman Catholic Bishop Frank Wood and Rabbi Solomon Frank in a public meeting at the Royal Alex Hotel to discuss the “essential unity underneath our divisions.” He then went a wee step further and had Rabbi Frank preach the sermon at a morning service at Westminster. Probably a first for the Rabbi and for a Christian Church in Canada. On another occasion he was a speaker at a mass rally at the Civic Auditorium (now the Manitoba Archives Building.) He says there were 4000 people at the rally to protest Nazi persecution of the Jews. His address was entitled I Speak For The Jew .
