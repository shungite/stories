When I read the dark and bloody records of what, in these very days … is happening in Nazi lands, I think not of Jews and jewesses: I think of men and women and children … and when I plead for the Jew I plead for my own family, and yours, and the families of all mankind.” He also participated in the founding of the Winnipeg branch of the Canadian Conference of Christian and Jews (now the Council, not the Conference.) The Conference had been established under the guidance of United Church Clergyman Claris Wilcox(an American of Congregationalist background.) Silcox was not warmly regarded in some parts of the United Church for his leadership in separating the Religious from the secular. Another story, though. Howse with collaboration from Sidney Smith, President of the University of Manitoba and fellow U.C. clergyman Dr. Ernie Hunter set up an organization. § The people who respected Howse’s stand on behalf of Jewish refugees were, however, perplexed at his stand on the partitioning of Palestine to create the nation state of Israel. The plan, put forward by the U.N. in 1947 was to split Palestine into two states, one for Arabs and one for Jews. The reader is invited to comb through the plethora of articles on the internet dealing with this still-contentious issue. Basically the Jews said yes. The Arabs said no. And the world was invited to take sides. The side that Howse took was on the side of Arab Palestine. The term that came to be used to describe this stand was anti-Zionism. We pay particular attention to two major commentators in Canada whose stand became strongly on the side of the Arabs. A.C. Forrest, editor of the influential United Church Observer, at the time, constantly criticized Israel’s policies and strongly supported the Palestinian cause, a position that led to a serious dispute with the Canadian Jewish community. Anti-Zionism they said.
