I quote liberally from this address not only for its information but also for its lavish praise of the young minister. “I do not need to recite Mr. Christies’ record as a student at the University of St. Andrews and The United Presbyterian College. It will be enough simply to remind you that it was brilliant. He comes crowned with the highest honours which his university could bestow; so that he does not belong to that class of men of whom the best and most charitable thing that could be said is “Clever fellows if they only would exert themselves …” As to what the congregation could expect of the man himself the following words: “… in the freedom of social intercourse — above all in helpfulness towards the lonely and forlorn and unfortunate — towards the flotsam and jetsam of humanity.” Mr. David Christie M.A. was seen as one who was alert to the needs of others. “He has insight and moral dignity of one whose life has not been free of bereavement and suffering.” The speaker continues, seeming to allude to the death of both of David’s parents when he was very young. David and Margaret’s Wedding certificate refers to both his parents as deceased. As to David’s preaching: “… it has not the hard metallic ring that mars the utterances of some who make pretensions of scholarship. It is penetrating — it has intimacy and inwardness — it has the lyric cry … etc.” Murray concludes his florid depiction, “David … does not belong to that body of men … who believe in inspired ignorance …” So, he was a clever fellow who could exert himself and he did not deal in ignorance, inspired or otherwise. Neither lazy nor stupid. I often wonder (perhaps cynically) at speeches such as this; what was Murray trying to accomplish? The congregation had already accepted David as their minister. Did they really need this kind or reassurance or did Murray simply see an opportunity to blow people away with his own spellbinding ability as a speaker? Self aggrandizement disguised as selfless approbation. Perhaps, on the other hand, it was the style of the time, something to be expected.
Rev. DAvid christie
