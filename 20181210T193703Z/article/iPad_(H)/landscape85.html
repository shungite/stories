Stories of Westminster United Church & its People / Page 85
By chance a prominent American cleric/missionary, Rev George Sherwood Eddy, recently retired, was seriously concerned about the development of American isolationism. Looking for a means of countering that development he hit on the idea of conducting travelling seminars to Europe. Given Eddy’s stature the type of people he would attract to his events were looking for a learning, not a tourist experience. The first of these seminars covered many of the major countries in continental Europe and it’s developing political ideologies. In 1927 he gathered his followers in London and headed for Russia. Bonnell joined the group. In a sense, for him the experience was life changing. While Eddy’s seminar would cover a wide range of social and economic topics, for Bonnell the compelling issue was an acute awareness of the Communist repression of organized religion. He was aware of the more immediate danger that was German Naziism. He was cognoscente of ltaly’s adventures in Fascism and he would, in time, show some contempt for socialism. However it was Russia that became the theme of many of his sermons, many of his weekly talks with various groups in Westminster, and the topic of a number of very successful public lectures he gave during his time in Winnipeg. He never forgot the Marxian slogan ‘Religion, the opiate of the people,’ a scrawl he had seen on a fence in St. Petersburg. When he later went on to New York to preach at Fifth Avenue Presbyterian Church he became a prominent voice in the shaping of American paranoia toward the dangers of the infiltration of Communism into the American social fabric. While American concern about this danger broadened to encompass the whole of American society and its institutions Bonnell’s focus was the danger to the Christian faith. Bonnell The Preacher ‘‘I grew up in Charlottetown and left school in 1908 when I was 15 before completing Grade 8. I was thoroughly bored by the subjects taught in the public school. The only subject that interested me was public speaking at which I quite excelled.” Bonnell was an exceptional speaker given to flights of rhetoric not uncommon among ministers in his day. He could project passion and peace, anger and benevolence, zeal and restraint.
