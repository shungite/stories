Bonnell — A Man of Two Countries, A Voice In His Time The first four men who ministered to the Westminster Congregation all hailed from Nova Scotia, the cradle of Canadian Scottish Presbyterianism. All were accounted as excellent preachers. All four ministries were each, in his own terms, successful. Charles Bruce Pitblado launched us in 1892 and later retired to the warmth of California. Clarence MacKinnon saw us through a period of incredible growth and left us to return to Nova Scotia where he became the head of Pine Hills Theological College. David Christie participated in the construction of our present sanctuary and shepherded his Presbyterian congregation into the newly formed United Church of Canada. From these three, a solid beginning. However it was the fourth minister, John Sutherland Bonnell, who in his working lifetime, attracted the most attention, wielded the most extensive influence, and received both the greatest praise, and the harshest criticism. His period of Westminster service, 1929 to 1935, was all within the decade of the Great Depression. It was also a period of European unrest that would eventually impinge on the Americas, Asia and the rest of the world. The Year Was 1929 The World was not yet Marshall McLuhan’s Global Village. Nevertheless we had the marvels of the telegraph and the telephone, and newspapers which kept us abreast of the world beyond our own. By the 1940s going to the movies inevitably entailed a Newsreel detailing the fighting overseas before the main show. Fast, efficient communication was drawing us together. Where Westminster’s second minister, Clarence McKinnon had crossed the Atlantic many times by sailing ship and the uncertain power of wind, by 1929 for David Christie our third minister passage across all earth’s seas was by steam and coal in large , comfortable, vessels. The reality of connectedness as we know it today was in its infancy but our awareness of things beyond was being sharpened. World War One, the “war to end all wars,” was already eleven years behind us. Governments in Europe, Asia and North America were looking at a variety of ways to meet the needs of their different social orders.
