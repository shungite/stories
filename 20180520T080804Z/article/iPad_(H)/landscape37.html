Yet by the time the Westminster congregation looked for new quarters in 1910, 17 years later, the logical place to go was the burgeoning area where many of Westminster’s people were now already living. By moving to Wolseley, Westminster would have also attracted to its congregation other residents of the Gates, Crescentwood, and Wolseley wishing to take advantage of their proximity to the newly erected church. § Back to the Hargrave days of Westminster. In 1899, there were 262 congregation members and counting; much of this growth being due to the popularity of Westminster’s evening services. Early in February, Reverend Pitblado was granted a six month-leave of absence — ill health again. He and Mrs. Pitblado embarked on what the family records and our own call “a trip around the world.” The congregation and a large portion of the general public were treated to letters back from the Pitblados as the tour unfolded. We have an interesting “news” item from a Hawaiian publication, the Hawaiian Weekly : “The Rev. C.B. Pitblado, pastor of one of the leading Presbyterian Churches in Winnipeg, Canada now in Honolulu on his return from India, China and Japan, having visited the missions of his own denomination, especially in India, and having made careful inquiries into the work of missionaries, bears testimony of the great improvement in sentiments touching Christianity. In towns and villages where a few years ago the Evangelists were refused admission, they are now welcomed by the natives; and in Government official reports where their work was in the beginning sneered at, there are now frequent kind and most favorable references to the salutary results of their preaching and their charitable institutions.” § The Pitblados returned to Westminster later that year in September. During the remaining four years of Charles’ ministry, the Pitblados attended two meeting of the Presbyterian Church’s General Assembly, one in Halifax and the other in Ottawa. Charles’ penchant for religious intellectualism  was given its due vent when, nearing the end of his life, he was awarded a Doctor of Divinity from Gale College in Galesville, Wisconsin.
